Foundations of Dynamic Systems · Project 1

Model & Simulate a Car Suspension

Time to put the whole course to work on one real machine. A car suspension is a second-order system you can feel every day — and tuning it is a genuine engineering trade-off between a comfortable ride and a car that grips the road. You'll build the model, drive it over bumps, and discover why there's no single "best" shock absorber.

The mission Model a quarter of a car as a two-mass system, simulate it driving over a bumpy road, and use the simulation to find a shock-absorber setting that balances ride comfort against road holding. Along the way you'll see resonance, damping, and state-space all show up in one place.

Step 1 · Build the model

The quarter-car: two masses, three springs of behavior

We model one corner of the car. The sprung mass ms is the body resting on the suspension; the unsprung mass mu is the wheel, tire, and hub. Between body and wheel sits the suspension — a spring ks and the shock absorber (damper) cs. Between wheel and road sits the tire, which is itself a stiff spring kt. The road height zr is the input that shakes everything.

Writing Newton's law (Lesson 2) for each mass gives two coupled equations:

ms z̈s = −ks(zs−zu) − cs(żs−żu)   // body: pushed only by the suspension
mu z̈u = ks(zs−zu) + cs(żs−żu) − kt(zu−zr)   // wheel: suspension above, tire below

Four states capture everything: body position and velocity, wheel position and velocity — x = [ zs, żs, zu, żu ]. That's a 4th-order, state-space system exactly like Lesson 7, and it has two natural modes: a slow body bounce near 1.3 Hz and a fast wheel hop near 11 Hz.

Step 2 · Drive it over bumps

Feel what the shock absorber does

Here is the corner of the car, driving over a bumpy road. Tune the shock damping, the speed, and the body mass, and watch the body ride. With too little damping the body floats and wallows long after each bump; with too much it slams stiffly over every ripple. Try matching your speed so bumps arrive near the body's 1.3 Hz bounce — with low damping it'll resonate and heave.

shock preset:

The two readouts are your engineering scorecard. Comfort is the RMS vertical acceleration of the body — what your stomach feels; lower is smoother. Road holding is how much the tire's grip force fluctuates — lower means the tire stays pressed to the road and steers and brakes reliably. Watch how no single damping wins both at once.

Step 3 · Find the trade-off

Comfort and handling want different shocks

Now sweep the damping across its whole range and plot both scores at once (computed from the model's frequency response to a standard rough road). Comfort bottoms out at fairly soft damping; road holding wants it firmer. The honest answer is a compromise band in the middle — which is exactly where real passenger cars are tuned.

This is the heart of the project: there is no single optimum, only a defensible compromise. Sports cars slide the marker right (firmer — grip and control over comfort); luxury cars slide it left (softer — comfort over ultimate grip). Adaptive dampers cheat by moving the marker in real time. The engineering isn't finding "the answer" — it's choosing where on this curve your machine should live.

Key takeaways

  • A car corner is a two-mass, 4th-order system with a slow body-bounce mode and a fast wheel-hop mode.
  • The shock absorber sets the damping ratio — the single most important ride-tuning parameter.
  • Comfort (RMS body acceleration) is minimized at soft damping; too little or too much both hurt.
  • Road holding (tire-force variation) wants firmer damping to kill wheel hop.
  • There is no single best setting — real design picks a compromise on the trade-off curve.
  • The same model, in state-space form, is what a real active-suspension controller runs on.

Investigate further

  1. Resonance hunt. In the ride simulator, set damping soft and find the speed where the body heaves the most. Explain, using the body's ~1.3 Hz bounce frequency and the bump spacing, why that speed is special.
  2. Mass effect. Increase the body mass. What happens to the body-bounce frequency and to comfort? Relate it to ωₙ = √(ks/ms).
  3. Two optima. From the trade-off curve, read off roughly where comfort is best and where handling is best. Why do they fall at different damping values? (Hint: which resonance does each metric care about?)
  4. Design call. You're tuning a family SUV and a track car with the same hardware. Where on the trade-off curve do you place each, and what real-world consequence justifies your choice?
Show discussion & answers

1. The body resonates when bumps arrive at its natural bounce frequency, ~1.3 Hz. If bumps are spaced a distance d apart, they arrive at frequency (speed ÷ d). Setting speed ÷ d ≈ 1.3 Hz makes each bump's push land in step with the body's rise-and-fall, so the motion accumulates — pure resonance (Lesson 6). With soft damping there's little to bleed the energy away, so the heave grows large.

2. Heavier body → lower bounce frequency (ωₙ = √(ks/ms) falls). A heavier body is harder to accelerate, so on a given road the RMS body acceleration usually drops (a smoother-feeling ride) — but the car sits and reacts more slowly, and the same softer, lower-frequency motion can feel wallowy. It also shifts which road speeds excite resonance.

3. Comfort is best at soft damping (ζ ≈ 0.15–0.2); road holding is best firmer (ζ ≈ 0.4–0.5). They differ because each metric is dominated by a different mode: comfort is about isolating the body from the road (soft suspension isolates better, up to the point where the body-bounce mode itself starts ringing), while road holding is about controlling wheel hop (the fast mode), which needs more damping to suppress. Different resonances, different optimal damping.

4. The SUV goes toward the soft end — passengers value a smooth, quiet ride, and modest grip loss is acceptable at family-driving limits. The track car goes toward the firm end — a race driver needs the tire planted for maximum cornering and braking grip and will accept a harsh ride to get it. The consequence is concrete: soften the track car and it'll float and lose grip over mid-corner bumps; stiffen the SUV and every expansion joint jars the passengers. Same curve, opposite priorities.
