Why the Laplace Transform Was Invented

Differential equations are hard because calculus is hard. The Laplace transform is a trick that makes them easy: it turns calculus into ordinary algebra. Once you see why anyone would want that — and what the mysterious variable s actually means — the whole machinery of control engineering opens up.

The one idea to hold onto

It turns calculus into algebra

Solving an ODE means doing calculus: integrating, matching initial conditions, wrestling with derivatives. The Laplace transform sidesteps all of it with a single move — under the transform, differentiating becomes multiplying by s. A derivative, the hardest operation in the room, becomes a multiplication a child could do.

You have already met this trick in another form. Multiplying big numbers is tedious, so we take logarithms: log turns × into +, we do the easy addition, then undo the log. Laplace does the identical favour for calculus.

Hard worldThe transformEasy worldThen undo
multiply numberslogarithmadd (×→+)anti-log
solve an ODELaplacealgebra (d/dt→×s)inverse Laplace

So the workflow is always the same three steps: transform the equation into the s-world, solve the algebra there, then transform back to get the answer in time. The middle step is where all the pain disappears.

The definition, and the one property that matters

d/dt becomes ×s

The transform itself is an integral that re-weights a time signal by the exponential e−st:

X(s) = ∫0∞ x(t) e−st dt   // "how much of the exponential e^{st} lives in x(t)"

You will almost never compute this by hand — that's what tables are for. What you must remember is the magic property that falls out of it, using integration by parts:

ℒ{ ẋ } = s X(s) − x(0)
ℒ{ ẍ } = s2 X(s) − s·x(0) − ẋ(0)
// with the system starting at rest, simply: ẋ → sX, ẍ → s²X

Watch the mass–spring–damper collapse into algebra

Take the equation you built in Lesson 2, m·ẍ + c·ẋ + k·x = F, and transform every term (starting at rest). Each derivative just drops an s:

(m s2 + c s + k)·X(s) = F(s)  →  X(s) = F(s)ms2 + cs + k

The differential equation became a fraction. No calculus survived. And look at the denominator — it's exactly the characteristic polynomial from Lesson 3. Its roots (the values of s that make it zero) are the poles, and they are the same λ's you found by guessing eλt. Laplace didn't invent new physics; it gave the poles a home: the s-plane.

The s-plane and the signal it stands for

The variable s is a complex frequency: s = σ + jω. Every point in the complex plane names one exponentially-changing signal, est = eσt·(cosine at ω). The real part σ sets growth or decay; the imaginary part ω sets the oscillation frequency. Drag the point (or use the sliders) and read the signal it represents on the right. A pole sitting at that point means the system's natural response contains this signal.

Drag the teal point in the s-plane, or use the sliders. Its mirror below the axis is the conjugate pole that real signals always come in pairs with.

The single most useful fact in control engineering lives in this picture. Left half-plane (σ < 0) → the signal decays → stable. Right half-plane (σ > 0) → it grows without bound → unstable. Right on the imaginary axis (σ = 0) → it oscillates forever, never settling. Stability is simply: are all the poles in the left half?

Where the mass–spring–damper's poles live

Now the payoff. The mass–spring–damper's two poles are the roots of m·s² + c·s + k. With m = 1 they sit at s = −ζωₙ ± jωₙ√(1−ζ²). Change the damping and stiffness and watch them move — and watch the impulse response on the right change in lock-step. The poles always ride a circle of radius ωₙ; the angle off the axis is set by ζ.

Read the connection straight off the plane: the poles' distance left of the imaginary axis (−ζωₙ) is the decay rate — further left means faster settling. Their height (ωₙ√(1−ζ²)) is the ringing frequency — higher means faster oscillation. Push ζ up and the pair swings down onto the real axis: two real poles, no oscillation, over-damped. This one diagram is why engineers "design by moving poles," the whole subject of the lessons ahead.

σ < 0 → stable (decays) σ > 0 → unstable (grows) on jω axis → pure oscillation poles = roots of denominator circle radius = ωₙ

Key takeaways

  • Laplace turns calculus into algebra: under the transform, ẋ → sX and ẍ → s²X (at rest), so an ODE becomes a polynomial equation.
  • The workflow is transform → solve algebra → invert — the same favour logarithms do for multiplication.
  • Transforming the mass–spring–damper gives X(s) = F(s)/(ms² + cs + k). The denominator is the characteristic polynomial; its roots are the poles.
  • s = σ + jω is a complex frequency. σ sets growth/decay, ω sets oscillation. Each point names one signal est.
  • Pole location is destiny: left half-plane = stable/decaying, right half = unstable, imaginary axis = sustained oscillation.
  • The poles are the same λ's from Lesson 3 — Laplace just gave them the s-plane to live in.

Quick check

Under the Laplace transform (starting at rest), what does d²x/dt² become?

s²·X(s). Each derivative multiplies by s, so the second derivative multiplies by s². This is exactly why a second-order ODE turns into a second-degree polynomial in s.

A system has a pole at s = +0.5. Is it stable?

No. A positive real part means the corresponding signal is e+0.5t, which grows without bound. Any pole in the right half-plane makes the system unstable.

Two poles sit exactly on the imaginary axis at ±j3. What does the response look like?

A pure, undying oscillation at ω = 3 rad/s — no growth, no decay. σ = 0 means zero damping (ζ = 0), so energy just circulates forever, like the closed loop in Lesson 3's phase portrait.

Exercises

  1. Calculation. Transform the first-order equation τ·ẋ + x = u (at rest) into the s-domain and write X(s)/U(s). Where is its single pole?
  2. Calculation. For m = 1, c = 4, k = 3, write the denominator polynomial and find the two poles. Is the system stable? Does it oscillate?
  3. Calculation. A second-order system has ωₙ = 5 and ζ = 0.6. Give the pole locations s = −ζωₙ ± jωₙ√(1−ζ²).
  4. Design. A machine settles too slowly and rings too much. In terms of where its poles sit, state what you want to change about their position, and connect each move back to a physical parameter (m, c, or k).
Show worked solutions

1. Transform: τ·sX + X = U, so (τs + 1)X = U and X(s)/U(s) = 1/(τs + 1). The pole is where the denominator is zero: τs + 1 = 0 → s = −1/τ. It's a single real pole on the negative real axis — pure first-order decay with time constant τ, exactly matching Lessons 1 and 3.

2. Denominator: s² + 4s + 3 = 0. Factor: (s + 1)(s + 3) = 0 → s = −1 and s = −3. Both are real and negative, so the system is stable and does not oscillate (no imaginary part) — it's over-damped, a sum of two decaying exponentials. (Check: ζ = c/(2√(km)) = 4/(2√3) ≈ 1.15 > 1.)

3. σ = −ζωₙ = −0.6·5 = −3. ωd = ωₙ√(1−ζ²) = 5·√(1−0.36) = 5·√0.64 = 5·0.8 = 4. So the poles are s = −3 ± j4. (Their magnitude is √(9+16) = 5 = ωₙ, as it must be.)

4. "Settles too slowly" means the poles are too close to the imaginary axis → you want to push them further left (more negative real part, −ζωₙ), which means more damping c and/or a design that raises ζ. "Rings too much" means the poles sit too high and too vertical (large imaginary part, small angle to the jω axis) → you want to rotate them toward the real axis, again by raising ζ (more c, or lowering the k/m ratio relative to c). The unifying goal: move the poles left and down toward the real axis. Increasing c does both at once, which is why the damper is the cleanest tuning knob — the same conclusion as the shock-absorber design in Lesson 2, now stated in the s-plane.
