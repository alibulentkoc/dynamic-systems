The Single Cylinder

Before circuits with many actuators, master the one at the heart of them all. A hydraulic cylinder turns oil flow into motion and oil pressure into force — and because a rod sticks out of only one end, it does those two jobs differently going out than coming back. Understanding that asymmetry is the foundation of every circuit that follows.

The mission Build a mental model of a single-cylinder circuit — pump, relief valve, directional valve, cylinder — and learn the two master equations: flow sets speed and pressure sets force. Then discover why a differential cylinder extends slow-and-strong but retracts fast-and-weak.

Step 1 · The two master equations

Flow → speed, pressure → force

A cylinder is an area A the oil pushes on. Everything follows from two facts. The flow Q pouring in has to fill the volume the piston sweeps, so the piston speed is flow ÷ area. The pressure P acts over that same area to make force:

speed   v = Q / A   // litres per second ÷ area = metres per second
force   F = P · A   // pressure × area = newtons

Now the twist: a differential cylinder has two different areas. The full bore (cap side) is area Ac; the rod steals some area on the other side, leaving the smaller annulus Aa = Ac − Arod. Push oil into the cap to extend; push into the rod side to retract. Same pump, two different areas — so two different speeds and two different forces.

Step 2 · Run the circuit

Extend, retract, and watch the pressure find the load

Here's the circuit: a fixed-flow pump, a relief valve that caps the pressure, a directional valve you switch, and the cylinder driving a load. Extend and retract it, change the pump flow and the load, and watch two things: the piston moves faster on the smaller area, and the pressure rises only as high as the load demands — until the load is too heavy, the relief valve blows off, and the cylinder stalls.

valve:

Notice the pressure gauge: on a light load the system runs at low pressure (it only makes the force it needs); on a heavy load the pressure climbs. Hit the relief setting and the excess oil dumps back to tank — the gauge pins, the cylinder stops, and you're just heating oil. A hydraulic system makes force on demand, not a fixed force.

Step 3 · The differential asymmetry

Fat rod = faster, weaker retract

Change the rod diameter and watch the extend and retract numbers split apart. Because retract uses the smaller annulus area, the same pump flow makes the piston move faster coming back — but that smaller area also makes less force at the same pressure. Extend is the mirror image: slow but strong. The ratio of the two is exactly the ratio of the areas.

v = Q / A F = P · A extend: slow & strong (Ac) retract: fast & weak (Aa) relief caps pressure → caps force power = P · Q

Key takeaways

  • Flow sets speed, pressure sets force: v = Q/A and F = P·A are the two equations behind every hydraulic circuit.
  • A differential cylinder has two areas: the full cap Ac and the smaller rod-side annulus Aa.
  • Extend is slow & strong; retract is fast & weak, with the speed and force ratios both equal to Ac/Aa.
  • Pressure rises to meet the load — no more. The relief valve caps it, which caps the available force and causes stall when the load is too heavy.
  • Return flow differs from supply flow because the two sides have different areas — on retract the cap-side return actually exceeds pump flow.
  • These fundamentals scale directly to the multi-cylinder circuits ahead.

Investigate further

  1. Speed ratio. A cylinder has a 50 mm bore and a 28 mm rod. By what factor is retract faster than extend?
  2. Force from pressure. With that cylinder at 150 bar, what force does it make extending vs retracting?
  3. Stall. Explain what physically happens when the load needs more force than the relief valve pressure can provide. Where does the pump's energy go?
  4. Design. A machine must push hard while extending but return quickly. Explain why a differential cylinder is naturally suited to this, and one situation where you'd instead want a double-rod (symmetric) cylinder.
Show discussion & answers

1. Ac = (π/4)·50² = 1963 mm²; Arod = (π/4)·28² = 616 mm²; Aa = 1963 − 616 = 1347 mm². Retract/extend speed ratio = Ac/Aa = 1963/1347 ≈ 1.46× faster.

2. Extend F = P·Ac = 150×10⁵ Pa × 1963×10⁻⁶ m² ≈ 29.5 kN. Retract F = P·Aa = 150×10⁵ × 1347×10⁻⁶ ≈ 20.2 kN. The extend force is larger by the same 1.46× ratio.

3. The pressure rises until it hits the relief setting, but that's still not enough force (F = P·A) to move the load, so the piston stalls. The pump keeps delivering flow, which now has nowhere to go but over the relief valve back to tank — all that hydraulic power (P·Q) turns into heat. Stalling against the relief is a common cause of hydraulic systems overheating.

4. A differential cylinder extends on the large cap area (slow, high force — good for the working push) and retracts on the small annulus area (fast, lower force — fine for a no-load return), which is exactly a "push hard, return quickly" duty. You'd choose a double-rod / symmetric cylinder when you need equal speed and force in both directions — for example a servo actuator whose control behaviour must be identical extending and retracting, so the dynamics don't change with direction.
