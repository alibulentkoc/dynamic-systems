Orders of a System

Zeroth, first, second, third. The “order” of a system is one of the most useful numbers in all of engineering — it tells you, before you solve anything, how the thing will respond to a push. Here we build that intuition one order at a time, with live traces you can drive yourself.

The one idea to hold onto

Order = how many things store energy

Every dynamic system responds the way it does because it can store energy and release it over time. Count the independent energy-storage elements and you have the order. That single count shows up three equivalent ways:

Physics view — the number of independent energy stores (a moving mass, a compressed spring, a charged capacitor, oil under pressure, water in a tank).
Math view — the order of the highest derivative in the system’s differential equation.
Systems view — the number of poles in the transfer function (we meet poles properly in a later lesson — here just note the count matches).

Our physical anchor for the whole lesson is a stack of water tanks, each draining into the next. One tank stores water one way, so it’s first order. Add tanks and you add stores — and add order. It’s the cleanest picture of what “order” means, and it happens to be pure fluid power.

Amber = inflow you command · teal = water stored · the level of the last tank is what you measure.

0
Zeroth order · algebraic

No memory at all

A zeroth-order system has no energy storage, so it has nothing to remember. The output is just the input scaled by a constant, instantly. Change the input and the output changes in the same breath — no lag, no settling, no overshoot.

y(t) = K · u(t)   // output = gain × input, right now

Every symbol: u = input you apply y = output you measure K = the gain (how much output per unit input). Turn K up and the output step gets taller; that is the only thing that can change, because there is no time behavior to shape.

Bench experiment · the lever

A ruler on the table edge is a lever — a pure zeroth-order machine. Push the free end down and the other end lifts at the same instant, by the input times the lever ratio. Drag the push distance and the ratio: the output moves in perfect lock-step, no lag ever. The right panel plots output vs input — always a straight line through the dot, because y = K·u with nothing in between.

Try it at home: press a ruler's overhang down 1 cm — the far end jumps up instantly. That instant, memory-free response is zeroth order.

1
First order · one energy store

The exponential approach

Give the system one place to store energy — one tank of water, one capacitor, the thermal mass of a cup of coffee — and it can no longer jump. It chases the target, fast at first (big gap) then easing in. The result is the famous exponential rise.

τ dydt + y = K u   // step response:   y(t) = K(1 − e−t/τ)

The new symbol is τ = the time constant (seconds) — the single number that sets the pace. After one τ the output has covered 63% of the way; after about 5τ it is essentially there. Bigger τ → slower, more sluggish system. Drag it below and watch the curve stretch or snap.

Bench experiment · the draining bottle

Poke a hole near the bottom of a bottle and the water level falls fast when full (lots of pressure) and slows as it empties — a first-order exponential. Widen the hole and it drains quicker; use a fatter bottle and it drains slower. Both change the same thing: the time constant τ. Watch the level drop and the curve on the right rebuild itself.

Idealized as a linear tank, so the drop is a clean exponential and τ = (tank area)×(flow resistance). A real sharp-edged hole follows Torricelli (∝√h) and empties in a finite time; a thin tube gives the textbook exponential shown here.

2
Second order · two energy stores

Where oscillation is born

Two energy stores that can trade energy back and forth — a mass (kinetic) and a spring (potential) — and suddenly the system can overshoot and ring. This is the most important order in engineering: suspensions, circuits, structures, and the closed loop of almost every controller behave second-order.

1ωn2 d²ydt² + 2ζωn dydt + y = K u

Two new symbols do all the work: ωn = natural frequency — how fast it wants to oscillate ζ (zeta) = damping ratio — how much the oscillation is bled away. Watch what ζ does:

ζ = 0 — undamped, rings forever 0 < ζ < 1 — underdamped, overshoots then settles ζ = 1 — critically damped, fastest with no overshoot ζ > 1 — overdamped, sluggish like two first-orders
Mass–spring–damper Car suspension RLC circuit Pendulum (small swing) Two tanks in series
Bench experiment · the twanging ruler

Clamp a ruler to the desk, overhang most of it, and twang the free end. It vibrates at a set pitch and the vibration dies away — a textbook second-order impulse response. Slide it out to overhang more and the pitch drops fast (natural frequency falls as 1/length²). Add damping and the ringing fades sooner. The beam is shown in slow motion so you can see the cycles; the right panel plots the true tip motion.

A twanged ruler is a cantilever beam: ωₙ ∝ √(stiffness/mass) and, for a fixed ruler, that works out to ωₙ ∝ 1/length². Doubling the overhang quarters the pitch.

3
Third order · three energy stores

Delay stacked on oscillation

Add a third store — often an actuator that can’t respond instantly (a valve spool, a motor’s inductance, a third tank) — and its lag piles on top of the second-order behavior. The response now starts slowly (an S-shaped toe) before it rings. This is the honest model of a real machine, where the thing doing the pushing has dynamics too.

Y = K ωn2s² + 2ζωns + ωn2 · 1τa s + 1 U   // 2nd-order plant × actuator lag

Same ωn and ζ as before, plus one more: τa = actuator time constant — how slow the “pusher” is. Turn τa up and notice the output no longer leaves the gate immediately: there’s a soft toe at the start. That built-in delay is exactly what makes third-order systems harder to control.

Three tanks in series DC motor → position Hydraulic servo (valve + cylinder + load) Actuator + mass–spring
Do it yourself Line up three cups with small holes, pouring top-to-bottom. Pour a steady stream into the top cup and watch the bottom cup. Its level barely moves at first (water is still working through the upper two), then rises in an S-curve. That slow toe is the signature of a third-order system — and it’s the classic model of a hydraulic servo axis.

Key takeaways

  • Order = number of independent energy stores = order of the highest derivative = number of poles. Count the stores and you know a lot before doing any algebra.
  • 0th order can’t lag, 1st order can’t overshoot. Overshoot and oscillation need two stores that can trade energy — that’s why second order is the star.
  • ζ (damping) decides the personality of a second-order system: ring, overshoot, critical, or sluggish.
  • A third store adds delay — an S-shaped toe — which is why real actuated machines (hydraulic servos, motor axes) are harder to control than the textbook mass-spring.
  • Higher orders are almost always lower-order pieces stacked in series. Learn 1st and 2nd cold and the rest is combination.

Quick-check quiz

1 · What does a system's "order" count, physically?

The number of independent energy stores. Equivalently, it's the order of the highest derivative in the equation of motion, and the number of poles. Count the stores and you know the order before any algebra.

2 · Why can a first-order system never overshoot?

It has only one energy store, so there's nothing for that energy to trade with. Overshoot and oscillation require two stores passing energy back and forth (e.g. a mass and a spring). One store can only ease toward its target — the exponential approach.

3 · A mass on a spring — what order, and why?

Second order. Two independent stores: kinetic energy in the moving mass and potential energy in the stretched spring. They exchange energy, which is exactly what produces oscillation.

4 · Which parameter sets a second-order system's personality — ringing vs. sluggish?

The damping ratio ζ. ζ < 1 rings (underdamped) with overshoot, ζ = 1 is critically damped (fastest with no overshoot), ζ > 1 is overdamped and sluggish.

5 · Why is a hydraulic servo axis third-order and harder to control than a textbook mass-spring?

It carries an extra energy store — the compressible oil / valve-flow dynamics — stacked on the mass-and-spring. That third store adds an S-shaped delay toe to the response, and delay is what makes a loop harder to keep stable.

Exercises

C1 · A car body bouncing on its suspension springs (ignore the tire) — how many stores, what order?

Two stores: kinetic energy in the body mass and potential energy in the suspension spring → second order. This is the simplest ride model.

C2 · Now include the tire's own springiness and the unsprung wheel mass — what order?

Four stores: body mass, suspension spring, wheel (unsprung) mass, and tire spring → fourth order. That's the quarter-car model from Project 1 — two coupled second-order systems.

D1 · Design: a step response shows a slight S-shaped toe before it rises. What minimum order, and what causes the toe?

At least third order. A pure first- or second-order response starts rising immediately; an S-shaped toe (slow start, then acceleration) betrays a third energy store adding delay — for example compressible oil in a hydraulic actuator or an extra filter/lag in the loop.
