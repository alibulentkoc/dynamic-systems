The Single Cylinder Circuit

This begins a five-part fluid-power sequence. We start at the atom of hydraulics: one pump, one valve, one cylinder, one load. Two simple laws — flow sets speed, pressure sets force — explain everything a cylinder does, including the surprising fact that a single-rod cylinder extends and retracts at different speeds and different maximum forces.

The mission Build the basic power-and-actuator circuit, drive the cylinder against a load, and discover the two governing relationships (Q = A·v and F = P·A) — then see why the rod makes extend and retract fundamentally asymmetric, and what the relief valve does when a load is too heavy.

Step 1 · The two laws

Flow makes speed; pressure makes force

A hydraulic cylinder is astonishingly simple to reason about. Oil is nearly incompressible, so whatever volume flow Q the pump pushes into a chamber must show up as the piston sweeping out that same volume. With piston area A, that fixes the speed. And pressure P acting on that same area produces force. Two equations run the whole show:

v = Q / A   // piston speed = flow ÷ area
F = P · A   // piston force = pressure × area

The pump is a flow source (it delivers roughly constant Q); pressure is whatever it takes to move the load, and rises until the job is done — or until the relief valve caps it and dumps the extra oil back to tank. So a cylinder's speed is set by the pump, but its pressure is set by the load.

Step 2 · The rod breaks the symmetry

One side has a rod; the areas differ

Here is the twist that trips up newcomers. The cap side is a full circle of area Acap, but the rod side is a circle with the rod bored out — an annulus of area Aann = Acap − Arod, which is smaller. Same pump flow into a smaller area means a faster stroke; the same relief pressure on a smaller area means less force. So:

extend: v = Q/Acap (slow), Fmax = P·Acap (strong)
retract: v = Q/Aann (fast), Fmax = P·Aann (weak)

Notice what the gauges tell you: pressure only climbs as high as the load demands. A light load barely raises the gauge; a heavy load drives it up to the relief setting, at which point the cylinder stalls and the pump's flow spills over the relief valve as wasted heat. And because the annulus area is smaller, retract always stalls at a lower load than extend — the cylinder is genuinely weaker pulling in than pushing out.

Key takeaways

  • Two laws run a cylinder: speed v = Q/A (flow ÷ area) and force F = P·A (pressure × area).
  • The pump sets speed; the load sets pressure. Pressure rises only as far as needed, up to the relief limit.
  • The relief valve caps pressure and dumps excess flow as heat when a load is too heavy — the cylinder stalls.
  • A single-rod cylinder is asymmetric: extend is slow and strong (Acap); retract is fast and weak (Aann).
  • Regeneration ports rod oil back to the cap side for a very fast but weak extend (effective area = Arod).
  • The area ratio Acap/Aann sets both the speed ratio and the force ratio between strokes.

Exercises

  1. Speeds. A cylinder has a 63 mm bore and 36 mm rod, fed by 30 L/min. Find the extend and retract speeds.
  2. Forces. With a 210 bar relief setting, find the maximum extend and retract force for that cylinder.
  3. Stall. A load resists motion with 60 kN. Using your Exercise 2 numbers, will the cylinder extend? Will it retract? Explain the difference.
  4. Design. A machine must extend slowly with high force to press a part, then retract quickly to save cycle time. Explain why a single-rod cylinder is naturally suited to this, and when you might instead reach for regeneration or a double-rod (symmetric) cylinder.
Show worked solutions

1. Acap = π/4·(0.063)² = 3.117×10⁻³ m²; Arod = π/4·(0.036)² = 1.018×10⁻³ m²; Aann = 2.099×10⁻³ m². Q = 30 L/min = 5.0×10⁻⁴ m³/s. Extend v = Q/Acap = 0.160 m/s ≈ 160 mm/s; retract v = Q/Aann = 0.238 m/s ≈ 238 mm/s (about 1.49× faster).

2. P = 210 bar = 2.1×10⁷ Pa. Extend F = P·Acap = 2.1×10⁷·3.117×10⁻³ ≈ 65.5 kN; retract F = P·Aann = 2.1×10⁷·2.099×10⁻³ ≈ 44.1 kN.

3. Extend: 60 kN < 65.5 kN, so it extends (needs ~192 bar, under the relief). Retract: 60 kN > 44.1 kN, so it stalls — the annulus area can't make 60 kN even at full relief pressure. Same cylinder, same pressure limit, but the smaller retract area makes it weaker pulling in. This is the single-rod asymmetry in action.

4. Extending on the large cap area gives the highest force for a given pressure and the slowest speed — exactly what a pressing stroke wants. Retracting on the small annulus area is naturally faster (and it needn't be strong, since it's just returning), which shortens the cycle. So the geometry hands you "slow-strong out, fast-return" for free. Use regeneration when you want the extend itself to be fast and the load is light (e.g., rapid approach before contact). Use a double-rod cylinder when you need equal speed and force both ways — for example a machine axis that must behave identically in both directions.
