Foundations of Dynamic Systems · Lesson 2

Modeling with Newton's Laws

The last lesson showed that a second-order system overshoots and rings. But where does its equation actually come from? Here we build it from scratch with the one law you already trust — F = ma — and watch three physical parts (a mass, a spring, a damper) each contribute one term. By the end you can look at a real machine and write its equation of motion.

The one idea to hold onto

Newton's second law is just force bookkeeping

Modeling a mechanical system is nothing more than this: draw the moving part, add up every force acting on it, and set the total equal to mass times acceleration. Each physical component you attach contributes one, predictable force — so each contributes one term to the equation.

The mass resists acceleration. Its force is m·ẍ — this is the inertia term, and it's why nothing responds instantly.
The spring resists displacement. It pulls back proportional to how far you've stretched it: F = −k·x (Hooke's law). This is what stores potential energy.
The damper resists velocity. It pushes back proportional to how fast you're moving: F = −c·ẋ. This is what bleeds energy away and settles the motion.

A mass, a spring, and a damper together are the mass–spring–damper — the single most important model in mechanical engineering. A car's suspension corner, a building swaying, a sensor's diaphragm, even a robot joint: all of them are, at heart, this.

The free-body diagram

Every force on the moving mass

The trick is to isolate the mass. On the left is the physical system — wall, spring, damper, sliding block. On the right we cut the mass free and replace each attachment with the single force it exerts. That second picture, forces only, is the free-body diagram, and it's what we turn into an equation. Displacement x is measured from the resting position; motion is horizontal, so gravity plays no part.

Isolate the mass, then replace each attachment with the force it exerts. The spring and damper forces always point back toward rest; F(t) is what you apply.

Building the equation

Add the forces, set equal to m·ẍ

Newton says the sum of forces equals mass times acceleration. Reading the arrows above:

m ẍ = F(t) − k x − c ẋ   // inertia = applied − spring − damper

Move the spring and damper terms to the left, and the model appears in its standard form — the same second-order equation from Lesson 1, now with physical meaning bolted onto every term:

m ẍ + c ẋ + k x = F(t)

Every symbol, in plain English: m = mass (kg) — the inertia c = damping (N·s/m) — the resistance to speed k = stiffness (N/m) — the springiness x = displacement from rest. Increase m and the system gets sluggish; increase k and it snaps back harder and faster; increase c and the ringing dies away sooner.

The bridge back to ωₙ and ζ

Divide through by m and compare with the standard second-order shape. Two physical groupings pop out — the same natural frequency and damping ratio you met last lesson:

ωn = √k√m // stiffer or lighter → faster natural frequency
ζ = c2√(k m) // damping, scaled by how much there is to damp

This is the punchline of the lesson: ωₙ and ζ are not abstract knobs — they are built out of the mass, spring, and damper you can hold in your hand. Change a physical part and you change the response, predictably. The demo below lets you feel exactly that.

Bench experiment · the hanging bottle

Pull it, count the bobs, then dunk it

This is the classic experiment, made live. A water bottle hangs from a soft band; pull it down and release, and it bobs at its natural frequency. Count the bobs in ten seconds and you've measured ωₙ/2π. Now drag the dunk depth to lower the bottle into the bucket: the bobbing dies away far quicker — yet the pitch stays exactly the same. That's the whole point. Dunking adds drag, which raises c and ζ, but it doesn't touch m or k, so ωₙ = √(k/m) is unchanged. Damping and frequency are independent knobs.

Bench experiment · the bobbing bottle (interactive twin)

Hang a full water bottle from a rubber band, pull it down, and let go: it bobs at ωₙ/2π. Count the bobs in ten seconds to estimate the frequency. Now dunk the bottle into a bucket of water and the bobbing dies away far quicker — you've raised the damping c without touching the mass m or the band stiffness k. Drive all three and watch ωₙ hold steady while ζ and the ten-second bob count change.

Modeled as a linear damper (drag ∝ speed), so the decay stays a clean exponential. Notice ωₙ and f don't move when you dunk — only ζ does. For real numbers, lay a phone on the bottle and log its accelerometer; the bob frequency and decay rate read straight off the trace.

Key takeaways

  • Modeling = force bookkeeping. Isolate the moving mass, sum every force, set it equal to m·ẍ. That's the whole method.
  • Each part adds one term: mass → m·ẍ (resists acceleration), spring → k·x (resists displacement), damper → c·ẋ (resists velocity).
  • The result, m·ẍ + c·ẋ + k·x = F(t), is the second-order system from Lesson 1 with physical meaning on every coefficient.
  • ωₙ = √(k/m) and ζ = c/(2√(km)) — the response's personality is built directly from the hardware.
  • The same three-term bookkeeping, with different parts, gives the equations for circuits and hydraulic actuators — which is why one toolkit covers them all.

Quick check

Which physical part is responsible for a system being able to overshoot?

The spring — together with the mass. Overshoot needs two energy stores that trade energy back and forth (spring's potential ↔ mass's kinetic). A mass with only a damper (no spring) can't overshoot; it just coasts to a stop.

You double the mass and leave k and c alone. What happens to ωₙ and ζ?

ωₙ = √(k/m) drops by a factor of √2 (slower). ζ = c/(2√(km)) also drops by √2 (less damped, more ringing). So a heavier mass is both slower and bouncier — which is exactly why a loaded truck rides differently from an empty one.

Why does the spring force arrow always point back toward the dashed line?

Because the spring force is −k·x: the minus sign means it always opposes the displacement. Stretch right (x>0) and it pulls left; compress left (x<0) and it pushes right. It's a restoring force, always aimed at the equilibrium position.

Exercises

  1. Calculation. A mass–spring–damper has m = 2 kg, k = 50 N/m, c = 4 N·s/m. Find ωₙ and ζ, and classify the response.
  2. Calculation. For that same system, what value of c would make it critically damped?
  3. Calculation. Write the equation of motion for m = 0.5 kg, k = 8 N/m, c = 0, with an applied force F(t). What kind of motion results after a pull-and-release?
  4. Design. A camera on a drone jiggles after each gust, taking too long to steady. You can change the mount's stiffness and add damping material. Using ωₙ and ζ, explain a strategy to make it settle quickly without making it feel harsh, and name the trade-off you're managing.
Show worked solutions

1. ωₙ = √(k/m) = √(50/2) = √25 = 5 rad/s (≈ 0.80 Hz). ζ = c/(2√(km)) = 4/(2√(100)) = 4/20 = 0.2. Since 0 < ζ < 1, it's underdamped — it overshoots and rings a few times before settling.

2. Critical damping is ζ = 1, i.e. c = 2√(km) = 2√(2·50) = 2√100 = 20 N·s/m. That's the least damping that produces no overshoot — the fastest non-bouncy settle.

3. 0.5·ẍ + 8·x = F(t), or ẍ + 16·x = 2·F(t). With c = 0 there is nothing to remove energy, so after a pull-and-release the mass oscillates forever at ωₙ = √(k/m) = √16 = 4 rad/s — pure, undying sinusoidal motion. (Real systems always have a little damping, so it eventually stops.)

4. "Settles quickly without feeling harsh" is a request to raise ζ toward about 0.6–0.7 — high enough to kill the ringing fast, low enough to avoid the stiff, abrupt feel of critical/over-damping. Add damping material to raise c (raises ζ directly). If you instead stiffen the mount you raise k, which speeds ωₙ but lowers ζ (ζ = c/(2√(km))), making it ring more — so stiffness alone backfires. The trade-off you're managing: speed of response (ωₙ) versus smoothness of settling (ζ), and c is your cleanest lever on the latter. This is exactly how a real shock absorber is tuned.
