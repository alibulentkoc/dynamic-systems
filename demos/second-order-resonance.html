Second-Order Systems: Frequency, Damping, Resonance

Push a swing at just the right rhythm and a tiny effort builds into a huge arc. That's resonance — and it's the defining drama of second-order systems. This lesson is about what happens when you drive a system with a steady rhythm, why the response can blow up, and how damping is the only thing standing between a useful machine and a shattered wine glass.

The one idea to hold onto

Drive it with a sine, get a sine back — resized

Feed a linear system a steady sinusoid at frequency ω, and after the transient dies it responds with a sinusoid at the same frequency — but scaled in amplitude and shifted in time. How much it's scaled is the frequency response, and it's just the transfer function evaluated on the imaginary axis, s = jω:

amplitude gain at ω  =  |G(jω)| = ωn2√[ (ωn2 − ω2)2 + (2ζωnω)2 ]

Look at the denominator. When the drive frequency ω approaches the natural frequency ωₙ, the first term (ωₙ²−ω²) collapses toward zero, and the only thing left holding the denominator up is the damping term 2ζωₙω. If ζ is small, the denominator gets tiny and the gain shoots up. That is resonance: near ωₙ, only damping limits how big the response gets.

Interactive · driving through resonance

Sweep the rhythm, watch it balloon

The mass on the left is shaken by a steady force whose frequency you set with the drive slider. Its steady-state swing is exactly |G(jω)| times the input. Sweep the drive toward ωₙ and watch the amplitude balloon — then drop off past it. The curve on the right is that same amplitude versus frequency; the amber dot is where your drive currently sits. Now lower ζ and watch the peak turn into a spike.

Two things to feel here. Far below or above ωₙ the mass barely cares about the shaking. Right at ωₙ, a small push produces a large motion — the input's energy arrives perfectly in step with the motion, lap after lap, and it accumulates. And the only knob that caps the peak is ζ. This is why a wine glass shatters at its pitch, why soldiers break step on bridges, and why every real structure needs damping.

Damping runs the whole show

How much peak? Set by ζ alone

The height of the resonant peak is governed entirely by the damping ratio. For an underdamped system the peak sits at ωr = ωₙ√(1−2ζ²) and reaches a height of about 1/(2ζ). Engineers call that peak factor the quality factor Q ≈ 1/(2ζ):

ζ small → tall sharp peak (high Q, rings) ζ = 0.707 → the peak just vanishes ζ > 0.707 → no resonance, smooth rolloff

There's a famous crossover at ζ = 1/√2 ≈ 0.707. Below it, the system amplifies a band of frequencies (it resonates). At or above it, the gain only ever falls with frequency — no peak at all. This is why 0.707 is a favourite design target: fast response, flattest possible frequency response, no resonant amplification.

Interactive · the Bode magnitude curve

Frequency response, the engineer's plot

The standard way to show |G(jω)| is on log–log axes (decibels versus log frequency) — a Bode magnitude plot. Low frequencies pass at the DC gain; the curve peaks (or not) near ωₙ; then it rolls off at −40 dB per decade, the signature slope of a second-order system. Drop ζ below 0.707 and watch the resonant peak rise out of the curve.

Bench experiment Wet a fingertip and run it slowly around the rim of a wine glass. Below the right speed, nothing; hit the glass's natural frequency and it suddenly sings loudly — you've driven it at resonance. A swing works the same way: pump your legs at the swing's own rhythm and the arc grows with almost no effort. Both are your ζ being small and your drive frequency landing on ωₙ.

Why this is everywhere

Wine glass shattering at pitch Tacoma Narrows bridge Washing machine spin-up Car suspension (why shocks matter) Engine mounts Hydraulic line resonance MEMS & tuning forks

A washing machine is a lovely case: as the drum speeds up it passes through its resonance on the way to spin speed, which is the moment it shakes hardest — good machines add damping and mass to get through that band quickly. In fluid power, long oil lines and trapped compressible fluid form their own spring–mass resonances that can hammer a system apart; the fix, as always, is damping and careful design of ωₙ.

Key takeaways

  • Frequency response = |G(jω)|: drive a linear system with a sine and it returns a sine at the same frequency, scaled by |G(jω)| and phase-shifted.
  • Resonance happens when the drive frequency nears ωₙ: the denominator of |G(jω)| collapses and only damping limits the gain.
  • ζ controls the peak. Peak height ≈ 1/(2ζ) = Q; it sits at ωr = ωₙ√(1−2ζ²).
  • ζ = 0.707 is the crossover: below it the system resonates; at or above it the response only rolls off, with no peak.
  • Second-order systems roll off at −40 dB/decade above ωₙ — the Bode signature.
  • Resonance is why damping isn't optional: it's the difference between a tuned machine and a broken one.

Quick check

Why does pushing a swing at its natural frequency work so well?

At ωₙ your pushes arrive in step with the motion every cycle, so each small input adds energy that accumulates. With little damping to bleed it away, the amplitude builds far beyond what a single push could do — that's resonance.

A system has ζ = 0.9. Does it have a resonant peak?

No. Since 0.9 > 0.707, there is no resonant peak — |G(jω)| starts at the DC gain and only decreases with frequency. Resonant amplification requires ζ < 0.707.

Two systems have the same ωₙ but ζ = 0.05 versus ζ = 0.3. Which has the taller, sharper peak?

The ζ = 0.05 one. Peak height ≈ 1/(2ζ), so it reaches about 1/0.1 = 10× versus about 1/0.6 ≈ 1.7× — twenty times... no, roughly six times taller, and much narrower (higher Q). Less damping means a taller, sharper resonance.

Exercises

  1. Calculation. A system has ωₙ = 10 rad/s and ζ = 0.1. Find the resonant frequency ωr and the approximate peak gain.
  2. Calculation. What is the quality factor Q of a system with ζ = 0.02, and what does that number tell you physically?
  3. Calculation. Below what damping ratio does a second-order system show a resonant peak at all?
  4. Design. A drone camera mount resonates badly at the rotors' spin frequency, blurring video. You can change the mount's stiffness/mass (to move ωₙ) and add damping (to raise ζ). Give two independent strategies to kill the problem, and explain the trade-off each one carries.
Show worked solutions

1. ωr = ωₙ√(1−2ζ²) = 10·√(1−2·0.01) = 10·√0.98 ≈ 9.9 rad/s (barely below ωₙ, since ζ is tiny). Peak gain ≈ 1/(2ζ√(1−ζ²)) ≈ 1/(2·0.1) = ≈ 5×. A small drive at ~10 rad/s produces roughly five times the steady output it would at DC.

2. Q ≈ 1/(2ζ) = 1/(2·0.02) = 25. Physically, Q ≈ 25 means the resonant peak is about 25× the DC gain — a very lightly damped, sharply tuned system that rings for many cycles before settling (roughly Q cycles). Great for a tuning fork, dangerous for a bridge.

3. A resonant peak appears only for ζ < 1/√2 ≈ 0.707. At exactly 0.707 the peak degenerates to the DC value; above it, |G(jω)| is monotonically decreasing.

4. Strategy A — move ωₙ away from the rotor frequency by stiffening or lightening the mount (raise ωₙ) or softening/adding mass (lower ωₙ), so the drive no longer lands on the peak. Trade-off: a stiffer mount transmits more high-frequency buzz; a softer/heavier one sags and responds sluggishly, and you must be sure the rotor frequency can't sweep back onto the new ωₙ. Strategy B — add damping (raise ζ toward or past 0.707) so the peak flattens and can't amplify even if the drive sits on ωₙ. Trade-off: more damping means more energy lost as heat and a slightly slower, softer response. In practice engineers use both: shift ωₙ out of the operating band and add enough damping to tame whatever peak remains — exactly how real camera gimbals and engine mounts are designed.
