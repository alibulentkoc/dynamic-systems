The RLC Circuit

Put an inductor in series with the RC circuit and the same thing happens as adding a spring to a mass: a second energy store appears, and the voltage can overshoot and ring.

① The setup② Two states③ Build the equation④ Three regimes⑤ The response⑥ Takeaways

1 The setup

Where overshoot comes from.

The RC circuit could only charge toward the supply. Add an inductor and the current itself now carries energy — it resists changing, so it overshoots the mark, pumps the capacitor past the supply voltage, and the pair swap energy back and forth while the resistor bleeds it away.

2 Two states, not one

The reason a second-order system can ring.

Two stores means two states: the capacitor voltage vC and the inductor current iL. The state is the pair (vC, iL) — and the analogy is exact: inductance is mass, capacitance is spring compliance, resistance is the damper.

Why first-order systems never overshoot. With one state there is only one way to go: toward equilibrium, slowing as you approach. Overshoot needs somewhere to store momentum that carries you past the target. Two energy stores, two states — and suddenly the system can sail right by.

Two numbers is all it takes. Whatever the hardware — mass and spring, inductor and capacitor, oil and load — once you have ωn and ζ you know the whole response. That is why controls engineers talk in ζ and ωn rather than in kilograms and henries.

4 The three regimes

ζ alone decides the character of the response.

Here ωn = 1/√(LC) and ζ = (R/2)√(C/L). Notice the resistor's role has flipped from the RC circuit: there, more R meant slower charging; here, more R means more damping — it is the brake that stops the ringing. A low-resistance LC circuit rings almost forever, which is the basis of an oscillator; add resistance and it settles.

Full solution — all three regimes

The character is decided by the roots of s² + 2ζωns + ωn² = 0, namely s = −ζωn ± ωn√(ζ²−1).

Underdamped (ζ < 1) — complex pair, ringing at ωd = ωn√(1−ζ²):

x(t) = 1 − e−ζωnt[cosωdt + (ζ/√(1−ζ²))sinωdt]

Critically damped (ζ = 1) — repeated real root:

x(t) = 1 − e−ωnt(1 + ωnt)

Overdamped (ζ > 1) — two real roots s1, s2; the slower one dominates and it behaves almost first order:

x(t) = 1 − (s2es1t − s1es2t)/(s2 − s1)

Useful shortcuts: overshoot Mp = e−πζ/√(1−ζ²) (a function of ζ only), peak time tp = π/ωd, and 2% settling ts ≈ 4/(ζωn). Every closed form here was cross-checked against numerical integration of the original ODE.

Takeaways

  • The inductor is the mass. It stores energy in current and resists change — exactly the role inertia plays mechanically.
  • Same equation, new labels. LC v̈ + RC v̇ + v = V with ωn = 1/√(LC), ζ = (R/2)√(C/L).
  • R switched sides. In the RC circuit resistance slowed charging; here it damps ringing. Same component, opposite role.
  • Ringing is energy sloshing. L and C trade energy while R dissipates it; with R → 0 the ringing never stops — that is an oscillator.
Quick check

An RLC circuit rings badly after each switching event. Name two fixes and their side effects.

Raise ζ = (R/2)√(C/L). (1) Add series resistance R — simple, but it burns power and slows the circuit. (2) Raise C or lower L — this also drops ωn = 1/√(LC), so the circuit gets slower. This is the same trade as the mechanical lesson: you can damp the ringing or you can keep the speed, and the two pull against each other. It is exactly why snubber design is a compromise.
