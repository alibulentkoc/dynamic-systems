On/Off Control

The simplest way to steer a cylinder to a target is a solenoid valve that's either fully open one way, fully open the other, or shut. Cheap, rugged, and everywhere — but it can never sit still. Because the valve only knows "full speed" or "stop," the cylinder overshoots, reverses, overshoots again, and hunts forever around the target in a limit cycle.

The mission Position a cylinder with a three-position on/off (bang-bang) valve and watch it hunt. Learn why a real valve's switching delay guarantees a limit cycle, and how a deadband trades chatter for a wider band of acceptable error — the fundamental compromise of on/off control.

Step 1 · The control law

Three states: extend, retract, or hold

An on/off controller compares the piston position x to a target x*. If it's low, drive full-speed out; if it's high, drive full-speed back; if it's within a small deadband of the target, shut the valve:

x < x* − d  →  extend at +V   |   x > x* + d  →  retract at −V   |   |x − x*| ≤ d  →  hold
// V = Q/A is the only speed available — there is no "slow down near the target"

The catch: a real solenoid valve takes a few tens of milliseconds to switch, and oil keeps flowing during that lag. So the piston sails past the deadband edge before it can stop, then the controller reverses, and it sails past the other edge. The result is a self-sustaining oscillation — a limit cycle.

Step 2 · Watch it hunt

It never settles — it circles the target

Set a target and let it run. The cylinder charges to the setpoint, overshoots because of the valve delay, reverses, overshoots the other way, and locks into a steady buzz around x*. Widen the deadband and the buzz gets slower and larger in position but the valve switches less; narrow it and the valve chatters furiously. You cannot make it simply stop.

Every on/off system lives with this. Widening the deadband is the honest knob: it reduces valve wear and chatter at the cost of a larger dead zone where the piston is "close enough." The limit-cycle amplitude is roughly the deadband plus the distance the piston coasts during the valve's switching delay, V·τ.

Step 3 · When on/off is the right choice

simple, cheap, rugged only full-speed or stop always hunts (limit cycle) deadband trades chatter ↔ accuracy no smooth approach

On/off control is perfect when you just need to reach an end stop or toggle between fixed positions — a clamp, a gate, a tipping bucket. When you need to stop gently and sit precisely on an arbitrary target, you need a valve that can meter flow in proportion to the error — which is exactly the next project.

Key takeaways

  • On/off (bang-bang) control drives the cylinder at full speed toward the target and shuts off inside a deadband — there is no proportional slowdown.
  • It always hunts. Valve switching delay lets the piston overshoot the deadband, producing a self-sustaining limit cycle around the setpoint.
  • Limit-cycle amplitude ≈ deadband + V·τ (coast distance during the valve delay).
  • Deadband is the tuning compromise: wider = less chatter and valve wear, but a larger zone of acceptable error; narrower = tighter but buzzes hard.
  • Great for reaching end stops or fixed positions; poor for precise, gentle positioning — use proportional control for that.

1. Half-amplitude ≈ d + V·τ = 3 mm + (170 mm/s)(0.06 s) = 3 + 10.2 ≈ 13 mm. The coast during the valve delay dominates the deadband here.

2. Halving the deadband makes the limit cycle smaller in position but the valve switches roughly twice as often — higher chatter frequency and more valve wear. Tighter control costs valve life.

3. As d → 0 the amplitude shrinks toward the coast term V·τ but the valve switches as fast as it physically can, essentially continuously — extreme chatter that overheats and destroys the solenoid and wastes energy. Some deadband is mandatory.

4. Appropriate: driving a clamp or gate to a hard end stop, or toggling a diverter between two fixed positions — the limit cycle at the stop is harmless. Unacceptable: positioning a robot joint, a machine-tool axis, or a camera platform to an arbitrary precise point, where the constant buzz would blur the result and shake the machine.
