Newton's Cooling

A hot cup of coffee cools toward room temperature — fast while it's hot, slow as it nears the room. Same first-order machinery as the tank, but now the target isn't zero. The trick is to decay the difference from equilibrium.

① The setup ② The state ③ Build the equation ④ The equilibrium shift ⑤ The cooling curve ⑥ Takeaways

1 The setup

A phenomenon everyone has watched happen.

An object at temperature T sits in a room at ambient Ta. Heat leaks out at a rate proportional to how much hotter the object is than its surroundings — Newton's law of cooling. The bigger the gap, the faster it cools; as the gap closes, cooling slows to a crawl.

2 Choosing the state variable

Track the quantity the cooling law is written in.

The object stores thermal energy, and for a lumped body that energy is U=CthT — proportional to temperature. Since the cooling law speaks in temperature, the state is T(t). The thermal capacitance Cth plays the tank's cross-sectional-area role: it sets how much energy a degree of temperature represents.

4 The equilibrium shift — back to the tank

One substitution turns "decay to ambient" into "decay to zero."

Full worked solution

With θ = T − Ta, the equation is pure decay dθ/dt = −kθ, so θ(t) = θ₀e−kt where θ₀ = T₀ − Ta. Add ambient back:

T(t)=Ta+(T0−Ta)e−kt

Same structure as the RC's charge curve — an equilibrium plus a decaying error. Every "settles toward a value" first-order system wears this shape.

Takeaways

  • Temperature is the state. Stored thermal energy scales with T, and the cooling law is written in T.
  • Cooling drives the difference to zero, not the temperature. Only the gap T − Ta matters.
  • Shift to equilibrium and it's the tank again. θ = T − Ta gives dθ/dt = −kθ, solved as θ₀e−kt.
  • Full solution = equilibrium + decaying error: T(t) = Ta + (T₀ − Ta)e−kt, with τ = 1/k.
Quick check 1 — the gap that matters

Two identical mugs start at 90°C, one in a 20°C room, one in a 5°C fridge. Which cools faster at t = 0, and why?

The fridge mug. Initial rate is −k(T − Ta): its gap is 85°C vs 70°C, so it loses heat faster. Both share the same k and τ, but the larger gap means a steeper start.
Quick check 2 — reading the half-life

With k = 0.05 s⁻¹, how long until the excess temperature halves?

Half-life = ln2/k = 0.693/0.05 ≈ 13.9 s. So a 70°C excess falls to 35°C in ~14 s, to 17.5°C in ~28 s, and so on.
Exercise — the forensic estimate

An object found at 40°C in a 20°C room was 90°C when it started cooling with k = 0.05 s⁻¹. How long has it been cooling?

θ = T − Ta: now 20°C, started 70°C. So e−kt = 20/70 = 0.286 ⟶ t = −ln(0.286)/0.05 = 1.253/0.05 ≈ 25 s. (This is exactly the logic behind estimating time-of-cooling in forensics.)
