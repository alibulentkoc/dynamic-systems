Study Hours & Grades

The first hours of study move your exam score a lot; later hours move it less. That diminishing return — knowledge climbing toward a ceiling — is the same first-order shape as a capacitor charging, only with study hours in place of time.

A model, not a prophecy. Real learning has plateaus, breakthroughs, forgetting, sleep, and test-day nerves. This lesson captures the robust part — diminishing returns and the idea of a reachable ceiling — to reason about study, not to predict any individual's grade.

1 The setup

Big early gains, then polishing.

Open the book cold and the first few hours are gold: you learn the core ideas that most of the exam rests on. Keep going and each additional hour adds a little less — you're now chasing edge cases and polish. Meanwhile the grade you earn is just which band your score lands in: an A above 90, a B above 80, a C above 70. The question is how many hours it takes to cross each line.

2 Choosing the state variable

Track what you've actually mastered.

The state is your mastery of the material, which we read as an expected exam score K out of 100. What's different here is the independent variable: not clock time, but cumulative study hours h. First-order systems aren't only about time — the same math governs any accumulation with diminishing returns, and here the "clock" is hours-on-task.

4 The ceiling sets the grade you can reach

The twist: more hours can't beat a low ceiling — only a better approach can.

Your score climbs toward Kmax and levels off there. That ceiling isn't fixed ability — it's set by how you study: active recall vs re-reading, worked problems vs skimming, spacing vs cramming, and how solid your prerequisites are. If your ceiling sits at 85, piling on hours brings you close to 85 but never to an A. The fix isn't more hours — it's raising the ceiling with better methods (or the rate k, which gets you there sooner).

Full worked solution & hours-to-a-grade

With the gap G = Kmax − K, the law is pure decay dG/dh = −kG, so G(h) = G0e−kh. Adding the ceiling back:

K(h)=Kmax−(Kmax−K0)e−kh

Hours to reach a target score Gt (90 for an A):

h=−1k ln(Kmax−90Kmax−K0)

Valid only when the target is below Kmax. Defaults (K0=40, Kmax=95, k=0.08): C at ~9.9 hr, B at ~16.2 hr, A at ~30.0 hr — the last grade costs the most hours because the gap is smallest there.

Takeaways

  • Mastery is the state; the "clock" is study hours. First-order structure doesn't need time — any diminishing-returns accumulation qualifies.
  • Diminishing returns is the whole story. dK/dh = k(Kmax − K): each hour's gain shrinks as you near the ceiling.
  • The ceiling decides the reachable grade. If Kmax < 90, no number of hours yields an A — raise the ceiling with better methods, don't just add time.
  • Front-load effort. Early hours buy the most points; the last grade band is the most expensive because the gap is smallest.
Quick check 1 — which hours are worth most?

Starting at 40 with ceiling 95 and k = 0.08, do you gain more from hours 0–5 or hours 20–25?

Hours 0–5, by a lot. Gain rate is k(Kmax−K): about 4.4 pts/hr at the start vs under 1 pt/hr by hour 20. The early gap is large, so those hours convert to points most efficiently.
Quick check 2 — stuck below an A

You've studied 40 hours and plateaued at 84. What does the model say to change?

You're near a ceiling around 85 — more hours barely help. Raise Kmax by changing how you study (practice problems and self-testing over re-reading, target weak topics, space sessions). That lifts the whole curve; hours then translate into the A.
Exercise — budget your hours

K0 = 40, Kmax = 95, k = 0.08 /hr. You have 16 hours before the exam. Which grade band do you land in, and how many more hours would an A take?

K(16) = 95 − 55·e−0.08·16 = 95 − 55·e−1.28 = 95 − 55·0.278 ≈ 79.7 — just under a B, essentially a high C. An A (90) needs ~30 hours total, so ~14 more hours — and note those 14 hours buy only ~10 points, versus the ~40 points the first 16 hours bought. Diminishing returns in action.
