The Leaking Tank

A tank of area A leaks at a rate proportional to its water height. We choose the state, assemble the differential equation from conservation, and watch it solve itself as a decaying exponential — then generalize to growth.

Step 1 — What is the state variable?

The leak law is stated in terms of height: the outflow is Qout=0.2h. A guiding rule of modeling:

Pick the state that the physics is written in. Because the leak depends on h, the natural state is the height h(t) — not the volume. Volume works too, but it forces an extra conversion (Tab 3).

Why the coefficient is c/A, not c. Dividing the whole balance by the area folds the geometry into the rate. A wider tank (larger A) drains slower for the same leak law — the coefficient shrinks and τ grows. Slide A above and watch τ move.
Common mistake — writing an inflow that isn't there

A frequent first attempt is dVdt=V0−0.2V. Two problems:
• V0 is an initial condition (a starting amount), not an inflow rate. A sealed, leaking tank has inflow = 0. Initial conditions never appear inside the balance — they're applied afterward when solving.
• The leak is 0.2h, tied to height, not 0.2V. Getting the state right the first time avoids this entirely.

Full worked solution — separation of variables

Starting from dhdt=−kh, collect like variables:

dhh=−k dt

Integrate both sides:

lnh=−kt+C

Exponentiate, and let eC be the constant fixed by the initial condition. At t=0, h=h0:

h(t)=h0e−kt

Laplace route: sH(s)−h0=−kH(s) ⇒ H(s)=h0/(s+k), whose inverse transform is the same exponential. Both roads meet.

Quick check

If the coefficient were k=0.04 s⁻¹, roughly how long until the tank drops to about a third of its start?

One time constant, τ = 1/k = 25 s, drops h to 1/e ≈ 37% of h₀. So ~25 s reaches roughly a third. After ~17.3 s it's at half (the half-life ln2/k).

Same system, different state

What if we insist on volume as the state? Use the geometric link V=Ah. Substituting h=V/A into the leak law converts the coefficient:

dVdt=−0.2VA=−0.04V
The coefficient is identical. V=Ah is just a linear rescaling, so both variables share the same time constant τ=25 s. Only the vertical scale differs (by the factor A). The shape of the response is a property of the system, not of which yardstick you use.

The general first-order law

Every equation of the form below has an exponential solution. What the proportionality creates is the exponential character; what the sign of k decides is which way it goes.

dydt=ky⟶y(t)=y0ekt
k > 0  runaway — exponential growth. Population, compound interest, unstable poles.
k = 0  no restoring term — constant. The state just sits there.
k < 0  self-limiting — exponential decay. Our leaking tank, RC discharge, cooling.
