Grain Drying

Corn comes off the field near 21% moisture but must reach about 15% for safe storage. Push air through the grain and moisture leaves — fast at first, then slower as the kernels approach balance with the air. It is Newton's cooling for water: decay toward an equilibrium the air itself sets.

1 The setup

Wet grain, moving air, a storage target.

Freshly harvested corn holds around 20–22% moisture — too wet to store, since mold and spoilage set in above roughly 15–16%. A fan drives air up through the grain mass; each kernel gives up moisture to the passing air in proportion to how much wetter it is than the air can hold. Whether that air is heated (drying) or just cool aeration air, the same law governs: the bigger the gap above equilibrium, the faster water leaves.

2 Choosing the state variable

Track the water the grain is holding.

Each kernel stores water, and we measure that as moisture content M (percent of the grain's weight). The drying law is written in terms of that moisture, so the state is M(t). This is the exact analog of temperature in cooling and voltage in the capacitor — the stored quantity the loss law depends on.

We use wet-basis percent here, as growers do. Drying research usually writes the same model for the dimensionless moisture ratio (defined in §4), which strips out the units entirely.

4 The air sets the floor

The new twist: you cannot dry below what the air will allow.

Unlike ambient temperature (fixed by the room), the equilibrium moisture Me is a design choice — it's whatever moisture the grain would settle to in balance with the drying air, set by that air's temperature and relative humidity. Warm, dry air ⟶ low Me; cool, humid air ⟶ high Me. The grain decays toward Me and stops there. If Me sits above 15%, no amount of running the fan reaches storage moisture.

Full worked solution & time-to-target

With excess moisture φ = M − Me, the law is pure decay dφ/dt = −kφ, so φ(t) = φ0e−kt. Adding Me back:

M(t)=Me+(M0−Me)e−kt

To find when it hits a target Mt = 15%, invert:

t=−1k ln(Mt−MeM0−Me)

Valid only when Mt > Me. Default numbers: t = −(1/0.15)·ln(5/11) ≈ 5.3 hr.

Takeaways

  • Moisture content is the state. The kernel stores water; the drying law is written in M.
  • Drying is Newton's cooling for water. dM/dt = −k(M − Me) — loss proportional to the gap above equilibrium.
  • The air sets the floor Me. Warm, dry air lowers it; if Me ≥ 15% the grain can never reach safe storage.
  • Full solution + a stopping time: M(t) = Me + (M0 − Me)e−kt, and 15% is reached at t = −(1/k)·ln[(15 − Me)/(M0 − Me)].
Quick check 1 — is 15% reachable?

Your aeration air gives an equilibrium moisture of Me = 16%. Corn is at 21%. Can you reach 15% storage moisture?

No. The grain decays toward 16% and stops; 15% is below the floor the air supports. You'd need warmer or drier air to drop Me below 15% first.
Quick check 2 — how long to store-ready

M0 = 21%, Me = 10%, k = 0.15 hr⁻¹. Time to reach 15%?

t = −(1/0.15)·ln[(15−10)/(21−10)] = −(6.67)·ln(0.4545) = 6.67 × 0.788 ≈ 5.3 hr.
Exercise — overdrying costs money

Grain sold by weight: overdrying past 15% throws away water you could have sold. With Me = 10%, how far past the 15% crossing (in time constants) until the grain hits 13%, and why does this argue for stopping promptly?

MR at 15% = 5/11 = 0.455; at 13% = 3/11 = 0.273. Extra time = −(1/k)·ln(0.273/0.455) = −τ·ln(0.600) = 0.51τ ≈ 3.4 hr more. Every point below 15% is shrinkage loss (and wasted fan energy), so drying control aims to stop right at the target — a first-order set-point problem.
