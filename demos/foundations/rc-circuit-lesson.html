The RC Circuit

A capacitor charges through a resistor toward a source voltage, then bleeds back down when the source is removed. It is the leaking tank in electrical clothing — with one new twist: a driven response that settles to a non-zero steady state.

① The circuit ② The state ③ Build the equation ④ Charge & discharge ⑤ Steady state & τ ⑥ Takeaways

1 The circuit

A source, a resistor, a capacitor, a switch.

Close the switch and a source Vs drives current through a resistor R into a capacitor C. The capacitor voltage vC rises — quickly at first, then slower as it approaches the source. Open the switch (or short the source) and it discharges back down.

2 Choosing the state variable

Same rule as always: track the thing that stores.

A capacitor stores charge, and its voltage is vC=q/C. Because the resistor's current depends on that voltage, vC is the natural state — exactly as height was for the tank. The capacitor plays the role of the tank's cross-section: it sets how much charge (water) a given voltage (height) holds.

3 Building the equation from Kirchhoff's law

Conservation of charge around the loop replaces conservation of water.

Kirchhoff's voltage law says the source equals the sum of drops: Vs=vR+vC. The resistor drop is vR=iR, and the same current fills the capacitor: i=C dv/dt. Substitute and rearrange.

Full worked solution — charging and discharging

The driven equation dvdt=(Vs−v)/τ is solved by shifting to the error e=v−Vs, which obeys the pure-decay law dedt=−e/τ. So e(t)=e0e−t/τ, giving:

v(t)=Vs+(v0−Vs)e−t/RC

Charging from empty (v₀=0): v = Vs(1 − e−t/RC). Discharging (Vs=0): v = v₀ e−t/RC. This "shift to the equilibrium, decay the error" trick reappears in the cooling and drag lessons.

5 Steady state and the time constant

Where it ends up, and how fast it gets there.

Set dv/dt=0 in the equation and the steady state pops out: v∞=Vs. The capacitor eventually matches the source and no more current flows. The speed of arrival is set entirely by τ=RC: after 1τ it's 63% there, after 3τ about 95%, after 5τ effectively done. Bigger R or C ⟶ slower.

Design reading. Want a slow, smooth filter? Increase RC. Want a snappy response? Shrink it. Every first-order choice in electronics — debounce timers, smoothing filters, sample-and-hold — is really a choice of this one number.

Takeaways

  • The stored quantity is the state. A capacitor stores charge, so its voltage vC is the state — the electrical analog of water height.
  • KVL is the conservation law. Source = resistor drop + capacitor voltage, with i = C dv/dt closing the loop.
  • A source term shifts the equilibrium. Unlike the tank, the RC settles to v∞ = Vs, not zero. Shift to the error and it's pure decay again.
  • One number governs the timing. τ = RC: 63% at 1τ, ~95% at 3τ, done by 5τ.
Quick check 1 — reading τ

R = 10 kΩ, C = 1 mF, charging from empty toward 5 V. What's v at t = 10 s?

τ = RC = 10 s, so t = 10 s is exactly one time constant: v = 5(1 − e⁻¹) ≈ 5 × 0.632 = 3.16 V.
Quick check 2 — the tank connection

What must you set so the RC equation becomes identical to the leaking tank's dv/dt = −v/τ?

Set the source Vs = 0 (discharge). With no drive, the capacitor simply bleeds through R — the exact electrical twin of water leaking from a tank.
Exercise — a 3-bit settling spec

A sample-and-hold must settle to within 1% of the source before reading. In units of τ, how long must you wait?

Remaining error is e−t/τ. Set e−t/τ = 0.01 ⟶ t/τ = ln(100) ≈ 4.6. So wait ≈ 4.6τ (people often round to 5τ for the ~99.3% it gives).
