Drag & Terminal Velocity

Push a mass with a steady force through a viscous medium and it speeds up — but not forever. Drag grows with speed until it exactly cancels the drive. Where that balance lands is the terminal velocity, and how the mass gets there is, once again, first order.

① The setup ② The state ③ Build the equation ④ The force balance ⑤ The velocity curve ⑥ Takeaways

1 The setup

A constant push, a resisting drag that grows with speed.

A body of mass m is driven by a constant force F while a viscous drag bv opposes its motion — the faster it goes, the harder the medium pushes back. (Linear drag like this is the honest model for low-speed motion in a fluid, e.g. a bead settling in oil.) Early on, drag is tiny and the body accelerates hard; as speed builds, drag catches up.

2 Choosing the state variable

Newton's law is written in velocity's rate of change.

A mass stores momentum, p=mv, and the drag law depends on velocity. So the state is v(t). The mass m is the "storage size" here — the momentum stored per unit velocity — playing precisely the role area played for the tank and capacitance played for the RC.

4 The force balance sets the equilibrium

Terminal velocity is simply where acceleration stops.

Set dv/dt=0 in Newton's law and the net force vanishes: F=bvt, so vt=F/b. At that speed the drag has grown to exactly match the drive; nothing is left over to accelerate the mass, and it coasts at constant speed. This is the mechanical version of the RC reaching the source and the coffee reaching room temperature — an equilibrium found by balancing, then approached exponentially.

Full worked solution

Shift to the error from terminal, e=v−vt with vt=F/b. It obeys pure decay de/dt=−(b/m)e, so from rest (v0 = 0, e0 = −vt):

v(t)=Fb(1−e−bt/m)

Identical shape to the RC charge curve — a terminal value times (1 − e−t/τ), with τ = m/b. Every domain, one solution.

Takeaways

  • Velocity is the state. The mass stores momentum mv, and the drag law is written in v.
  • Newton's law is the balance. m·dv/dt = F − bv — drive minus a speed-dependent drag.
  • Terminal velocity is a force balance. Set acceleration to zero: vt = F/b, where drag exactly cancels drive.
  • Approach is first-order: v(t) = (F/b)(1 − e−t/τ) with τ = m/b — heavier mass or lighter drag → slower.
Quick check 1 — where it tops out

Drive F = 5 N, drag coefficient b = 0.5. What terminal velocity, and does mass change it?

vt = F/b = 5/0.5 = 10 m/s. Mass does not change the terminal value — only how long it takes to get there (τ = m/b). A heavier mass reaches the same top speed, just more slowly.
Quick check 2 — the RC connection

Which RC quantities do m, b, and F correspond to?

Mass m ↔ capacitance C (the storage), drag b ↔ conductance 1/R (the leak), and drive F/b ↔ source Vs (the target). τ = m/b matches τ = RC, and both curves are terminal·(1 − e−t/τ).
Exercise — sizing for a speed limit

You need terminal velocity of exactly 8 m/s with a drive of F = 4 N. What drag coefficient b achieves it, and what's τ if m = 6 kg?

vt = F/b ⇒ b = F/vt = 4/8 = 0.5. Then τ = m/b = 6/0.5 = 12 s, so it reaches ~63% of 8 m/s (≈5 m/s) in 12 s and is essentially at terminal by ~60 s.
