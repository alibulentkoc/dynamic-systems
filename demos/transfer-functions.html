Foundations of Dynamic Systems · Lesson 5

Transfer Functions

One ratio holds everything a linear system does: G(s) = output ÷ input. It's the system's fingerprint. Once you have it, you can predict any response, snap systems together like Lego, and read a machine's behavior off the map of its poles and zeros.

The one idea to hold onto

A transfer function is a system's fingerprint

In Lesson 4 the mass–spring–damper collapsed into X(s) = F(s)/(ms²+cs+k). Divide output by input and the input disappears, leaving a pure property of the system itself:

G(s) = Y(s)  outputU(s)  input   // e.g. mass–spring–damper:   G(s) = 1ms² + cs + k

Read the name literally: it transfers input into output. In the s-world you don't solve a differential equation — you just multiply: Y(s) = G(s)·U(s). Feed it any input and G(s) tells you the output. Everything the system can do is baked into this one object.

Poles and zeros: the two lists that say it all

Every transfer function is a ratio of two polynomials, G(s) = N(s)/D(s). The roots of each get names:

Poles — roots of the denominator D(s), where G blows up. These are the system's natural modes, set by its energy storage (Lessons 2–4). They decide stability and the shape of the transient: how fast it decays, whether it rings.

Zeros — roots of the numerator N(s), where G goes to zero. They shape how the input couples in — they can speed a response up and add overshoot, or, when they sit in the right half-plane, make the output lurch the wrong way first.

One number you can read instantly: the DC gain G(0), found by setting s = 0. It's the steady-state output for a unit step — where the response finally settles.

Snapping systems together

Block diagrams: series means multiply

The real power of transfer functions is that they compose. Draw each subsystem as a block and connect them, and the algebra is trivial: blocks in series multiply, blocks in parallel add, and a feedback loop becomes G/(1 + G·H). No differential equations to re-derive — just multiply the fingerprints.

series:   G(s) = G1(s) · G2(s)     parallel:   G1 + G2     feedback:   G1 + G·H

Key takeaways

  • The transfer function G(s) = Y(s)/U(s) is the complete fingerprint of a linear system. In the s-world, output = G(s) × input — multiplication, not calculus.
  • Poles are the denominator's roots — the natural modes that set stability and the transient's shape (decay, ringing).
  • Zeros are the numerator's roots — they shape how the input couples in: adding overshoot, or, in the right half-plane, causing the output to move the wrong way first (non-minimum phase).
  • DC gain = G(0) is where a step response settles.
  • Systems compose: series blocks multiply, parallel add, feedback is G/(1+GH). Cascading an actuator and a plant multiplies their denominators, adding poles and order.
  • Engineers design by placing poles and zeros — everything ahead is learning where to put them.

Quick check

What is the DC gain of G(s) = 5/(s² + 2s + 10), and what does it tell you?

Set s = 0: G(0) = 5/10 = 0.5. A unit step input settles to an output of 0.5 — that's the steady-state value the response climbs (and rings) toward.

Two identical first-order blocks 1/(τs+1) are put in series. What's the combined order?

Multiply them: 1/(τs+1)² — the denominator is now second degree, so it's a second-order system with two (repeated) poles. Series connection multiplies, so orders add: 1 + 1 = 2.

A step response dips downward before rising to its final value. What does that imply?

A zero in the right half-plane — a non-minimum-phase system. The output initially moves opposite to where it ends up. Real examples: a bicycle steering the wrong way to initiate a turn, or a boiler drum's water level dropping just after feedwater is added.

Exercises

  1. Calculation. A system has G(s) = 20/(s² + 4s + 20). Find its poles, its DC gain, and say whether it oscillates.
  2. Calculation. Write the poles and zeros of G(s) = 4(s + 2)/[(s + 1)(s + 10)]. How many of each?
  3. Calculation. Put G₁ = 2/(s + 2) in series with G₂ = 3/(s + 3). Write the combined transfer function and its DC gain.
  4. Design. A positioning stage is fast but overshoots too much. You're told you can add a real zero to the controller. Where would you place it (left or right half-plane, near or far from the origin) to trim the response, and what must you watch out for if you push it too far?
Show worked solutions

1. Poles solve s² + 4s + 20 = 0 → s = (−4 ± √(16−80))/2 = −2 ± j4. Complex with negative real part, so it's stable and oscillates (rings at ωd = 4 rad/s, decaying as e−2t). DC gain = G(0) = 20/20 = 1.

2. Zeros are numerator roots: s + 2 = 0 → one zero at s = −2. Poles are denominator roots: two poles, at s = −1 and s = −10. So: 1 zero, 2 poles — a proper transfer function.

3. Series → multiply: G = G₁·G₂ = 6/[(s + 2)(s + 3)] = 6/(s² + 5s + 6). DC gain = G(0) = 6/6 = 1. (Equivalently, G₁(0)·G₂(0) = 1·1 = 1 — DC gains multiply in series too.)

4. Add a zero in the left half-plane (negative real axis). A zero speeds the response and can be used to cancel or soften a sluggish pole; placing it moderately close to the dominant pole trims settling without wrecking stability. The caution: push the zero too close to the origin and it injects strong derivative action that increases overshoot and amplifies noise; and never let it drift into the right half-plane, which would make the stage lurch backward before moving forward (non-minimum phase). Practically: place it to partly cancel the slowest pole, then stop.
