Differential Equations, Read Physically

You built the equation m·ẍ + c·ẋ + k·x = F. Now the skill that matters most: reading what it says without solving it. The trick is to stop seeing an equation to be cracked and start seeing a rule for what happens next — and once you see that, the solution becomes something you can picture.

The one idea to hold onto

An equation of motion is a local rule

A derivative is just a rate: ẋ is how fast position changes right now, ẍ is how fast velocity changes. A differential equation ties those rates together into a single instruction: given exactly where you are this instant, here is which way — and how fast — you are about to move. Solving the equation is nothing more than following that instruction forward, one tiny step at a time. That's literally what every simulator on Earth does.

Watch how our mass–spring–damper equation becomes a rule. Solve it for the highest derivative:

ẍ = F − c ẋ − k xm   // "my acceleration now = leftover force ÷ my mass"

Read it aloud: my acceleration right now is whatever force is left after the spring (−k·x) and damper (−c·ẋ) take their share, divided by my mass. Give me your position and velocity this instant and I can tell you your acceleration — which updates your velocity — which updates your position — forever. That chain is the solution.

Interactive · an ODE is a field of instructions

The slope field, and following it

For a first-order equation ẋ = f(x), the rule assigns a slope to every point: "if you're here, head this way." Draw those slopes as little arrows and you get a slope field — a map of the equation's instructions. The solution is simply the curve that stays tangent to the arrows as it flows left to right. Drop a starting height, pick an equation, and watch the solution thread through the field. The amber dashed line shows what happens if you follow the arrows in clumsy big steps (Euler's method) — shrink the step and it hugs the true curve.

equation:

Three things to notice. The arrows never reference the solution — they belong to the equation alone; the curve just obeys them. Wherever the arrows go flat (slope zero), the system stops changing — that's an equilibrium. And the true solution is smooth because it responds to the field continuously; Euler only checks the field every Δt, so it drifts.

How many instructions to pin down the future?

Order = number of initial conditions

A first-order equation needs one starting number (where you begin). A second-order equation like the mass–spring–damper needs two: position and velocity. That's not a coincidence — it's the same count as the energy stores and the same count as the order. You cannot predict a swing's future from its position alone; you also need to know how fast it's already moving. Those two numbers are the system's state.

1st order → 1 start value 2nd order → position + velocity state = what you must know now

Interactive · the solution as a path in state space

The phase portrait

Since a second-order system's state is (position, velocity), we can plot those two against each other. As the system evolves, that point traces a path — the phase portrait — and the whole character of the solution shows up as a shape. The faint arrows are again the field of instructions, now in the (x, v) plane. Undamped, the path is a closed loop: energy circulates forever. Add damping and the loop becomes an inward spiral — every lap loses a little energy — until it reaches the origin, which is rest. Over-damp it and it slides straight home without a single loop.

This is worth sitting with, because it's the seed of state-space modeling (Lesson 7): a dynamic system is a point moving through a space of states, pushed along by the field its equations define. The time trace on the right and the spiral on the left are the very same solution, shown two ways.

Why exponentials keep showing up

The shape linear ODEs love

Here's a pattern you'll see everywhere. Guess that the solution has the shape x = eλt — pure exponential growth or decay — and substitute it into a linear equation. Every derivative just multiplies by λ, so the whole differential equation collapses into an ordinary polynomial in λ, called the characteristic equation. For the homogeneous mass–spring–damper:

m λ² + c λ + k = 0   // solve for λ; the roots decide everything

The roots λ are the punchline: real and negative → pure decay; complex → decay with oscillation (the imaginary part is the ringing frequency); positive real part → it blows up. You've just met, in disguise, the poles of a system — the stars of Lessons 4 and 5. Reading an ODE often comes down to: what are its λ's, and where do they sit?

Key takeaways

  • An ODE is a local rule. Solved for the top derivative, it says "given your state now, here's your rate of change" — and the solution is that rule followed forward in time.
  • The slope field is the equation made visible. The solution is the curve tangent to the arrows; flat arrows mark equilibria.
  • Solving numerically = stepping along the field. Smaller steps (Euler Δt) track the true curve more closely.
  • Order = number of initial conditions = size of the state. Second order needs position and velocity.
  • The phase portrait shows the whole solution as a shape in state space: closed loop (undamped), inward spiral (underdamped), straight-in (overdamped).
  • Linear ODEs love eλt. Substituting it gives the characteristic equation; its roots λ are the system's poles and decide decay, oscillation, or blow-up.

Quick check

Why can't you predict a pendulum's motion from its position alone?

Because it's a second-order system: its future needs two starting numbers, position and velocity. A pendulum passing through the bottom could be barely drifting or swinging hard — same position, completely different futures. You must know the velocity too.

In a slope field, what does a horizontal arrow mean physically?

Slope zero means ẋ = 0 — the rate of change is zero, so the system isn't moving at that value. It's an equilibrium. If nearby arrows point toward it, it's stable; if they point away, unstable.

Your phase portrait is a perfect closed loop that never shrinks. What's the damping?

Zero. A closed loop means energy is conserved every lap — nothing removes it — so c = 0 and ζ = 0. The instant you add damping, the loop becomes an inward spiral.

Exercises

  1. Calculation. A system obeys ẍ = (F − c·ẋ − k·x)/m with m = 2, k = 50, c = 4. Right now F = 0, x = 0.1, ẋ = −0.2. What is the acceleration ẍ at this instant?
  2. Calculation. For ẋ = −x/τ with τ = 2 s and x(0) = 3, roughly how long until x falls to about 1.1? (Hint: think in time constants.)
  3. Calculation. Find the characteristic equation and its roots for ẍ + 3·ẋ + 2·x = 0. What kind of motion do the roots predict?
  4. Design. You're shown a phase portrait that spirals inward, but the loops are still large after several laps. Using what the spiral's tightness means, describe what you'd change in the physical system to make it spiral into rest faster — and what the portrait would look like if you overdid it.
Show worked solutions

1. ẍ = (F − c·ẋ − k·x)/m = (0 − 4·(−0.2) − 50·(0.1))/2 = (0 + 0.8 − 5)/2 = (−4.2)/2 = −2.1 m/s². The spring (pulling back from x = 0.1) dominates the small damper help, so the mass accelerates back toward rest.

2. For ẋ = −x/τ the solution is x(t) = 3·e−t/τ, which falls by a factor of e every τ seconds. After one time constant, x = 3/e ≈ 1.10. So the answer is about τ = 2 s. (This is exactly the "fall to roughly a third" rule from the bottle demo.)

3. Guess x = eλt: each derivative brings down a λ, giving λ² + 3λ + 2 = 0. Factor: (λ + 1)(λ + 2) = 0, so λ = −1 and λ = −2. Both roots are real and negative → the motion is a sum of two decaying exponentials with no oscillation: overdamped, settling straight to zero (the slower −1 root dominates the tail).

4. The tightness of the spiral is set by the damping ratio ζ: loops that barely shrink mean ζ is small. To spiral in faster, raise the damping c (adds the cleanest ζ without changing where it settles) — each lap then loses more energy and the spiral collapses quickly. Raising stiffness k speeds the circulation but actually lowers ζ = c/(2√(km)), loosening the spiral, so that backfires. If you overdo the damping (ζ ≥ 1) the portrait stops looping altogether: the path slides straight into the origin with no encirclement at all — critically or over-damped.
