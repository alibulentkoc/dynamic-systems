One Language: Mechanical, Electrical, Hydraulic

Here is the secret that ties the whole course together — and the reason a single toolkit runs robotics, power electronics, and fluid power alike. A mass on a spring, an RLC circuit, and a hydraulic cylinder are the same system. Same equation, same poles, same behavior, just with the labels swapped. Learn one and you have learned all three.

The one idea to hold onto

Every domain speaks in effort and flow

Under the hood, every physical domain has the same two quantities: an effort that pushes, and a flow that results. Their product is always power. And every domain has the same three ingredients: something that stores energy in motion (inertia), something that stores it in compression (compliance), and something that dissipates it (resistance).

Same equation, three costumes

Write the second-order law in each domain and the shapes are identical — an inertia term, a resistance term, a compliance term, equal to the driving effort:

m ẍ + c ẋ + k x = F  // mechanical
L q̈ + R q̇ + (1/C) q = V  // electrical (RLC), q = charge
I Q̇ + R Q + (1/Ch) ∫Q = P  // hydraulic

Line them up and the correspondence is exact: mass ↔ inductance ↔ fluid inertance; damping ↔ resistance ↔ valve loss; stiffness ↔ 1/capacitance ↔ bulk-modulus stiffness. Solve one, and the poles, the ωₙ, the ζ, the resonance — everything transfers, unchanged, to the others.

The fluid-power payoff

Trapped oil is a spring

This is why fluid power belongs to the same story. Oil looks incompressible, but it isn't quite: squeeze a trapped volume V and its pressure rises in proportion to its bulk modulus β. In a cylinder of piston area A, moving the piston by x compresses the oil and pushes back with a force — a spring force. Work it out and the effective stiffness is:

khyd = β A²V   // stiffer oil, bigger piston, smaller trapped volume → stiffer "spring"

So a hydraulic actuator driving a load mass m is a mass–spring–damper with ωₙ = √(khyd/m) — and it can ring and resonate exactly like the others. The villain of fluid power hides in β: dissolve a little air into the oil and the effective bulk modulus plummets, softening the spring, dropping ωₙ, and making the axis sluggish and bouncy. Managing β — bleeding air, controlling temperature — is half of making hydraulics feel crisp.

Key takeaways

  • Mechanical, electrical, and hydraulic systems obey the same second-order equation — an inertia, a resistance, and a compliance term equal to a driving effort.
  • Every domain has an effort and a flow (force/velocity, voltage/current, pressure/flow) whose product is power.
  • The analogies are exact: mass ↔ inductance ↔ fluid inertance; damping ↔ resistance ↔ valve loss; stiffness ↔ 1/capacitance ↔ βA²/V.
  • Trapped oil is a spring of stiffness k = βA²/V, so a hydraulic actuator has ωₙ, ζ, and resonance like any other second-order system.
  • Air entrainment lowers β, softening the hydraulic spring and dropping ωₙ — the classic cause of a spongy, sluggish axis.
  • One language covers them all, which is why a single toolkit runs robotics, power electronics, and fluid power.

Quick check

What is the electrical analog of a mechanical spring?

A capacitor. Stiffness k corresponds to 1/C (inverse capacitance): a stiff spring is like a small capacitor. Both store potential energy — the spring in compression, the capacitor in its electric field.

Why does dissolved air make a hydraulic axis feel spongy?

Air drastically lowers the oil's effective bulk modulus β. Since the hydraulic stiffness is k = βA²/V, a lower β means a softer spring, a lower ωₙ, and a slower, bouncier response — the axis loses its crispness.

You double a hydraulic cylinder's piston area. What happens to its stiffness?

It quadruples. k = βA²/V depends on area squared, so doubling A multiplies stiffness by four (and raises ωₙ by two, for a fixed load). Area is the strongest lever on hydraulic stiffness.

Exercises

  1. Calculation. An RLC circuit has L = 2 H, R = 4 Ω, C = 0.05 F. Write its characteristic polynomial in s (use Lq̈ + Rq̇ + q/C = V) and state which mechanical parameters L, R, 1/C correspond to.
  2. Calculation. A hydraulic cylinder has β = 1.4 GPa, piston area A = 20 cm², trapped volume V = 0.4 L. Find the hydraulic stiffness k = βA²/V (watch your units).
  3. Calculation. That cylinder drives a load of m = 800 kg. Estimate the natural frequency ωₙ and f in Hz.
  4. Design. A hydraulic press axis resonates and feels spongy near its working point. Using k = βA²/V and ωₙ = √(k/m), give three distinct physical changes that would raise ωₙ (stiffen the axis), and name the practical downside of each.
Show worked solutions

1. Dividing by L: q̈ + (R/L)q̇ + (1/LC)q = V/L. The characteristic polynomial is s² + (R/L)s + 1/(LC) = 0, i.e. s² + 2s + 10 = 0 (since R/L = 2, 1/LC = 1/(2·0.05) = 10). Correspondences: L ↔ mass m (inertia), R ↔ damping c, 1/C ↔ stiffness k. (Poles: s = −1 ± j3 — stable, underdamped.)

2. Convert units: A = 20 cm² = 20×10⁻⁴ m² = 2×10⁻³ m²; V = 0.4 L = 0.4×10⁻³ m³ = 4×10⁻⁴ m³; β = 1.4×10⁹ Pa. Then k = βA²/V = (1.4×10⁹)(2×10⁻³)²/(4×10⁻⁴) = (1.4×10⁹)(4×10⁻⁶)/(4×10⁻⁴) = (5.6×10³)/(4×10⁻⁴) = 1.4×10⁷ N/m (14 MN/m).

3. ωₙ = √(k/m) = √(1.4×10⁷ / 800) = √(1.75×10⁴) ≈ 132 rad/s, so f = ωₙ/2π ≈ 21 Hz. A stiff, fairly high-frequency resonance typical of a hydraulic axis.

4. From k = βA²/V and ωₙ = √(k/m): (a) Raise β — bleed entrained air and control oil temperature to keep bulk modulus high; downside: requires good maintenance and can't exceed the oil's intrinsic β. (b) Increase piston area A — the strongest lever since k ∝ A²; downside: a bigger cylinder needs more flow for the same speed and costs more. (c) Reduce trapped volume V — shorten hoses, minimize dead volume, mount the valve close to the cylinder; downside: less room for accumulators/cushioning and tighter packaging. (Also valid: reduce load mass m, though that's often fixed by the job.) Each raises ωₙ but trades against flow demand, size, cost, or serviceability — the real engineering balance in fluid power.
