Foundations of Dynamic Systems · Lesson 8

One Language: Mechanical, Electrical, Hydraulic

Here is the quiet secret this whole course has been building toward: a mass on a spring, an electrical circuit, and a hydraulic cylinder are, mathematically, the same system. Learn the second-order equation once and you can read all three — which is exactly why one engineer can move between robotics, electronics, and fluid power.

The one idea to hold onto

Different physics, identical equations

Every domain has the same three ingredients: something that stores energy in motion (inertia), something that stores energy in a squeeze (compliance), and something that dissipates energy (resistance). Two stores plus a dissipator gives a second-order system in any domain — same ωₙ, same ζ, same poles, same response. Only the names on the parts change.

The RLC circuit is a mass–spring–damper

Write the loop equation for an inductor, resistor, and capacitor in series (in terms of charge q, where current is q̇), and put it beside Newton's equation from Lesson 2:

mechanical:   m ẍ + c ẋ + k x = F
electrical:    L q̈ + R q̇ + (1/C) q = V
// identical form, term for term: m↔L, c↔R, k↔1/C, x↔q, F↔V

They are the same equation. So an RLC circuit has a natural frequency ωₙ = √(1/(LC)) and a damping ratio ζ = (R/2)√(C/L), it can resonate, it has poles in the same s-plane — everything you learned about the spring applies directly, with a straight substitution.

Into fluid power

The hydraulic cylinder is a spring

This is where the course points: physical AI runs on actuators, and the strongest are hydraulic. A cylinder seems rigid, but the trapped oil is slightly compressible, so it behaves like a stiff spring. Its stiffness comes from the oil's bulk modulus β, the piston area A, and the trapped volume V:

hydraulic spring:   kh = β A² / V    →   ωn = √(kh / m)   // stiffer oil, bigger piston, less trapped volume → higher ωₙ

The catch that ruins real machines: a few percent of air entrained in the oil can drop the effective β by three to ten times. The hydraulic spring goes soft, ωₙ plummets, and a servo that was crisp starts to ring and hunt. That's why fluid-power engineers obsess over bleeding air, minimizing trapped volume, and knowing exactly where their hydraulic resonance sits.

Key takeaways

  • One equation, three domains. Mechanical, electrical, and hydraulic second-order systems share the same form — same ωₙ, ζ, poles, and response.
  • The analogy map: mass ↔ inductance ↔ fluid inertance; spring ↔ capacitor ↔ oil compressibility; damper ↔ resistor ↔ valve.
  • An RLC circuit is a mass–spring–damper with m↔L, c↔R, k↔1/C, so ωₙ = √(1/LC).
  • A hydraulic cylinder is a spring of stiffness kh = βA²/V; its ωₙ = √(kh/m).
  • Air entrainment slashes bulk modulus β, softening the hydraulic spring and dropping ωₙ — the classic cause of a mushy, ringing hydraulic axis.
  • Because the math is shared, every tool in this course transfers to fluid power — the foundation for designing hydraulic actuators for physical AI.

Quick check

In the mechanical-to-electrical analogy, what plays the role of the spring stiffness k?

The reciprocal of capacitance, 1/C. A stiff spring (large k) corresponds to a small capacitor (small C). Both store potential energy when "squeezed" — one by displacement, one by charge.

Why does a hydraulic cylinder have a natural frequency at all — isn't oil incompressible?

Oil is only nearly incompressible. Its slight compressibility (bulk modulus β) makes the trapped column act like a stiff spring, kh = βA²/V. Spring plus load mass = a second-order system with ωₙ = √(kh/m), which can resonate.

An operator complains a hydraulic axis suddenly feels soft and bouncy. What's a likely cause?

Air entrained in the oil. Even a small air fraction can cut the effective bulk modulus several-fold, softening the hydraulic spring, lowering ωₙ, and making the axis ring and hunt. Bleeding the air restores stiffness.

Exercises

  1. Calculation. An RLC circuit has L = 0.1 H, C = 10 µF, R = 20 Ω. Find ωₙ = √(1/LC) and ζ = (R/2)√(C/L). Does it ring?
  2. Calculation. A hydraulic cylinder has β = 1.4 GPa, piston area A = 8 cm², trapped volume V = 0.4 L, load m = 150 kg. Find kh and ωₙ (using kh = βA²/V).
  3. Calculation. For that cylinder, air entrainment drops β to 0.35 GPa (a quarter). What happens to ωₙ?
  4. Design. You're designing a hydraulic joint for a walking robot and the axis resonates in its operating band. Using kh = βA²/V and ωₙ = √(kh/m), give three physical changes that would raise ωₙ out of the band, and note a downside of each.
Show worked solutions

1. ωₙ = √(1/(LC)) = √(1/(0.1·10×10⁻⁶)) = √(1/1×10⁻⁶) = √(10⁶) = 1000 rad/s (≈ 159 Hz). ζ = (R/2)√(C/L) = (20/2)·√(10×10⁻⁶/0.1) = 10·√(1×10⁻⁴) = 10·0.01 = 0.1. Since ζ = 0.1 < 0.707, it's underdamped and rings — an electrical resonator, identical in form to a lightly damped spring.

2. β = 1.4×10⁹ Pa, A = 8×10⁻⁴ m², V = 4×10⁻⁴ m³. kh = βA²/V = 1.4×10⁹·(8×10⁻⁴)²/(4×10⁻⁴) = 1.4×10⁹·6.4×10⁻⁷/4×10⁻⁴ = 1.4×10⁹·1.6×10⁻³ = 2.24×10⁶ N/m. ωₙ = √(kh/m) = √(2.24×10⁶/150) = √(14930) ≈ 122 rad/s (≈ 19 Hz).

3. kh scales directly with β, and ωₙ scales with √kh = √β. Cutting β to a quarter cuts kh to a quarter and ωₙ by √4 = 2. So ωₙ drops from ≈ 122 to ≈ 61 rad/s (≈ 9.7 Hz) — half the frequency, a much softer, ring-prone axis. This is why air entrainment is so damaging.

4. To raise ωₙ = √(βA²/(Vm)): (i) reduce trapped volume V — shorter lines, cylinder mounted closer to the valve (downside: less cushioning, tighter packaging, and ωₙ still varies over the stroke as V changes); (ii) increase piston area A — ωₙ ∝ A, a strong lever (downside: bigger, heavier, more flow demand and cost); (iii) keep β high by bleeding air and controlling temperature, or reduce the moving load mass m (downsides: air management is ongoing maintenance; lighter structure may be less rigid or lower payload). In practice engineers combine a stiff, compact cylinder (small V, adequate A) with clean, well-bled oil, then add damping to tame whatever resonance remains — the same "move ωₙ and add damping" playbook from Lesson 6, now in fluid power.
