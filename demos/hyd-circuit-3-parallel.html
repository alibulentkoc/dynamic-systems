Cylinders in Parallel

Feed two cylinders from the same supply line and you'd expect them to move together. They don't. Because a parallel connection shares one pressure, oil rushes to whichever cylinder is easiest to move — the lighter load extends fully first, and only then does the heavier one budge. It's the most famous gotcha in fluid power, and the reason flow dividers exist.

The mission Put two cylinders across one pump line and discover the rule of a parallel circuit: pressure is shared, flow divides — and unequal flow runs to the path of least resistance. Watch the light load win the race, then add a flow divider and force them to synchronize.

Step 1 · One pressure, split flow

Oil takes the easy path

In parallel, both cylinders' cap ports hang off the same node, so they see the same pressure. To move a cylinder against load F you need pressure P = F/A. The pump raises the line pressure until some cylinder can move — and that's the one with the lightest load, because it needs the least pressure. All the flow goes to it while the heavier one stays locked, waiting:

Pneeded = F / A  →  the smallest F moves first     then P rises to the next load, and so on   // shared pressure, flow to least resistance

Step 2 · Race the cylinders

Different loads → they sequence, not synchronize

Give the two cylinders different loads and start the flow. The lighter one shoots out first at full speed while the heavier one sits still; when the light one bottoms out, the pressure climbs and the heavy one finally moves. Now flip on the flow divider and watch them lock together — the divider forces an equal split regardless of load.

Without the divider, the shared-pressure rule guarantees sequencing: only the cylinder whose load matches the current line pressure can move. With the divider, each branch is forced to take half the pump flow, so both extend at the same speed and the pressures in each branch are free to differ to match their own loads.

Step 3 · Series vs parallel, side by side

The two ways to split a pump

These two circuits are mirror images, and knowing which is which is half of circuit design:

SERIES: shared flow · speeds coupled · pressures add PARALLEL: shared pressure · flow divides · least load moves first sync a parallel pair → flow divider sync a series pair → matched areas

Key takeaways

  • Parallel cylinders share one pressure and split the flow — the opposite of a series circuit.
  • The lightest load moves first. Oil follows the path of least resistance, so an unloaded cylinder extends fully before a loaded one begins — they sequence, they don't synchronize.
  • A flow divider forces an equal split, making both cylinders move together regardless of their loads.
  • Line pressure tracks the moving cylinder's load, rising step-by-step as each heavier cylinder takes its turn.
  • Series adds pressures and couples speeds; parallel shares pressure and divides flow. Pick the one whose coupling you want.

Investigate further

  1. Who moves first? Two parallel cylinders (same bore) carry 6 kN and 18 kN. Which extends first, and what line pressure is needed to start each?
  2. Speed. Without a divider, how fast does the moving cylinder go compared to the pump's rated cylinder speed? With a 50/50 divider?
  3. Fix it. A machine needs two parallel cylinders to lift a platform evenly. Why will a plain parallel circuit tip the platform, and what component fixes it?
  4. Contrast. In one sentence each, state what is shared and what divides in a series versus a parallel circuit.
Show discussion & answers

1. The 6 kN cylinder moves first. It needs P = F/A ≈ 6 000/19.63×10⁻⁴ ≈ 31 bar; the 18 kN cylinder needs ≈ 92 bar. The pump only reaches 31 bar first, so the light one runs to its end before pressure climbs to 92 bar and the heavy one starts.

2. Without a divider the single moving cylinder gets all the flow, so it runs at the full v = Q/A. With a 50/50 divider each gets Q/2, so both run at half that speed — but together.

3. A plain parallel circuit lets the least-loaded corner move first, so the platform lifts unevenly and tips. A flow divider (or two matched pumps, or a mechanical tie/rack) forces equal flow to each cylinder so they rise together.

4. Series: the cylinders share one flow stream (speeds are coupled) while pressures add. Parallel: the cylinders share one pressure while the flow divides between them.
