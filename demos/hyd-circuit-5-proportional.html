Proportional Control

Swap the on/off solenoid for a proportional valve — one that opens in proportion to a command — and everything changes. Now the controller can command a little flow when the error is small, so the piston eases smoothly onto the target and stops. This is the leap from a machine that hunts to one that positions, and it's the foundation of every servo-hydraulic axis in robotics and automation.

The mission Replace bang-bang with a proportional valve driven by u = Kp·(error). Discover that flow-in-proportion-to-error makes the closed loop a first-order system with a smooth, chatter-free exponential approach — then see how the gain Kp sets the speed, and how it compares directly against the on/off hunting of the previous project.

Step 1 · Meter the flow to the error

A little error → a little flow

A proportional valve delivers flow proportional to its command (up to a saturation limit). Drive that command from the position error, and the cylinder speed becomes proportional to how far it still has to go:

Q = Kp·(x* − x)     v = Q / A  ⟹  ẋ = −(Kp/A)·(x − x*)   // a first-order system → smooth exponential approach

That equation is the first-order "chase" from Lesson 1: the piston approaches the target exponentially with time constant τ = A / Kp, slowing as it arrives and settling exactly on x* with no chatter. Far from the target the commanded flow exceeds what the valve can pass, so it saturates at full speed (behaving like on/off) — then tapers smoothly once inside the linear range.

Step 2 · Smooth in, and compare to on/off

One knob: the gain Kp

Command a step and watch the proportional response ease onto the target. Raise Kp for a faster approach (shorter τ); lower it and it crawls in. Toggle the on/off trace to see the same move done bang-bang — it slams to the setpoint and buzzes forever, while proportional settles clean.

Proportional control buys smoothness and precision at the price of a valve that costs more and a loop you must tune. Crank the gain too high on a real actuator and the smoothness is spoiled by the hydraulic resonance you met in Project 2 — the compressible oil turns a too-aggressive loop into an oscillator. The art is a gain fast enough to be crisp yet below the resonance limit.

Step 3 · The control ladder

on/off: full-speed or stop → hunts proportional: flow ∝ error → smooth settle τ = A / Kp (raise Kp → faster) saturates far out, tapers near target gain limited by hydraulic resonance

Key takeaways

  • A proportional valve meters flow to the command, so commanding Q = Kp·error makes cylinder speed proportional to error.
  • The closed loop is first-order: ẋ = −(Kp/A)(x − x*), a smooth exponential approach with time constant τ = A/Kp and no chatter.
  • Gain Kp is the tuning knob: higher → faster (shorter τ); the command saturates far from target and tapers within the linear band.
  • Versus on/off: proportional settles cleanly on any target where bang-bang hunts in a limit cycle.
  • Too much gain excites the compressible-oil resonance (Project 2) and the loop oscillates — tune below the hydraulic natural frequency.
  • This is the basis of servo-hydraulic position control for robotics, machine tools, and physical-AI actuators.

1. τ = A/Kp = 19.6×10⁻⁴ / 8×10⁻⁴ ≈ 2.45 s. Settling (to ~2%) takes about 4τ ≈ 10 s. (Real servo valves use much higher gains for sub-second response.)

2. τ = A/Kp, so doubling Kp halves τ and roughly halves the settling time — twice as fast.

3. Far from target the commanded flow Kp·error exceeds the valve's maximum, so it saturates and the cylinder runs at full speed (just like on/off). Once the error is small enough that Kp·error drops below the valve's capacity, the valve meters proportionally and the motion tapers smoothly — the exponential tail.

4. Raising Kp pushes the closed-loop bandwidth up toward the hydraulic natural frequency ωₙ = √(kh/m) set by the oil's bulk modulus (Project 2). Past a limit the loop excites that resonance and the compressible oil makes the actuator ring and go unstable. Usable gain is capped below the hydraulic resonance — and air entrainment, which lowers ωₙ, lowers that ceiling further.
