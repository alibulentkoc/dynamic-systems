Wheel Speed Controller

Command a voltage to a drive wheel and the speed does not jump — it ramps. The rotor's inertia has to be spun up against friction and back-EMF, and the wheel eases into its commanded speed on a first-order curve every driver would recognise as throttle lag.

① The setup② The state③ The equation④ The response⑤ Takeaways

1 The setup

A robotics system that rises toward a set level.

A DC motor drives a wheel. Apply a step of voltage and torque accelerates the rotor; as speed rises, back-EMF cuts the current and friction grows, so the acceleration fades. The wheel approaches a steady speed proportional to the applied voltage.

2 Choosing the state variable

Track the stored quantity the rate law depends on.

The stored quantity is rotational kinetic energy in the rotor, read as angular velocity ω. Both friction and back-EMF depend on speed, so ω(t) is the state.

3 Building the equation

The gain per step shrinks as the state nears its target — the signature of first order.

Full solution & interpretation

Shift to the gap g = KV − ω; it obeys pure decay dg/dt = −k g, so g(t) = g₀e−t/τ. Undo the shift:

ω(t)=KV+(ω0−KV)e−t/τ

Steady speed is ω_ss = K·V, linear in the commanded voltage — that is your throttle map. The time constant is τ = J/(b + K_tK_e/R_a): rotor inertia over effective damping, where back-EMF adds damping on top of friction. Heavier rotor means more lag; a lower-resistance winding means stiffer, faster response.

Takeaways

  • Angular velocity is the state. The rotor stores kinetic energy; friction and back-EMF both scale with speed.
  • Throttle lag is first order. τ dω/dt + ω = KV — a voltage step gives an exponential ramp, not a jump.
  • Back-EMF is damping. τ = J/(b + K_tK_e/R_a): the motor's own generated voltage adds to friction and speeds up the settle.
  • Which term you drop matters. This is first order because armature inductance is negligible (τelec ≈ 1 ms vs τmech ≈ 1 s) — not because inertia is. Inertia is what creates τ.
Quick check

A source says you get this first-order model by "ignoring rotor inertia". What is wrong with that?

It is backwards. Setting J = 0 gives τ = J/b_eff = 0 — the speed would jump instantly to KV and there would be no dynamics at all. Inertia is precisely what makes the wheel lag. The term you actually neglect is armature inductance L_a: with τelec = L_a/R_a ≈ 1 ms against τmech ≈ 1 s, the current settles so fast it looks instantaneous, collapsing a second-order electromechanical system to this first-order one. Keeping L_a gives the full second-order model.
