Cylinders in Series

Connect the outlet of one cylinder into the inlet of the next and you build a series circuit — the same oil passes through both. That single fact has two big consequences: the cylinders' speeds are locked together by their areas, and the pump has to supply the sum of both loads. It's the classic way to (almost) synchronize two actuators from one pump.

The mission Feed the discharge of cylinder 1 into cylinder 2 (same-size cylinders) and discover the two rules of a series circuit: flow is shared (so speeds are coupled through the area ratio) and pressures add down the chain. Then see why "same size" cylinders don't perfectly synchronize — and what would fix it.

Step 1 · One stream, two cylinders

Shared flow couples the speeds

The pump pushes oil into the cap of cylinder 1. As its piston advances, it expels oil from its rod side — and that discharge is piped into the cap of cylinder 2, driving it forward. The two cylinders sit on one stream of oil, so their motions are locked together. Because the discharge comes off the smaller rod-side area Aa but fills cylinder 2 on the full cap area Ac, the second cylinder runs slower:

v1 = Q / Ac     discharge from 1 = Aa·v1     ⟹   v2 = v1 · (Aa / Ac)   // same-size cylinders ⇒ v₂ < v₁

Step 2 · Run the chain

Watch the flow pass through, and the pressures stack

Both loads and the pump flow are yours to set. Watch cylinder 2 trail cylinder 1 (they don't perfectly sync), and watch the pump-pressure gauge: it has to overcome load 2 through cylinder 1, so it reads higher than either load alone. Series connection makes the pump carry the sum.

The force balance tells the pressure story. Cylinder 2's load sets an intermediate line pressure Pm = F2/Ac; the pump then has to beat cylinder 1's load plus that back-pressure acting on the rod side:

Pm = F2 / Ac     Ppump = F1/Ac + F2·Aa/Ac²   // pressures add down the chain — the pump feels both loads

Step 3 · Why "same size" won't synchronize

Perfect sync needs matched areas

Toggle "area-matched" above and the two pistons lock step-for-step. The trick: make cylinder 2's cap area equal cylinder 1's rod-side area, Ac2 = Aa1. Then the discharge volume from 1 exactly fills 2 over the same travel, so v2 = v1. With two identical cylinders that condition can't be met (the rod-side area is always smaller than the cap), so the second always lags by the ratio Aa/Ac. Real synchronizing circuits size the cylinders deliberately down the chain.

series = one shared stream v₂ = v₁·(Aa/Ac) pressures add: Ppump feels both loads sync needs Ac2 = Aa1 contrast: parallel splits flow (next)

Key takeaways

  • A series circuit shares one flow stream, so the cylinders' speeds are coupled — you can't move one without the other.
  • Same-size cylinders don't perfectly synchronize: the second runs slower by the area ratio Aa/Ac, because it's filled by the smaller rod-side discharge.
  • Pressures add down the chain. The pump must supply cylinder 1's load plus the back-pressure from cylinder 2 — more than either load by itself.
  • True synchronization needs matched areas: size cylinder 2's cap to equal cylinder 1's rod side.
  • This is the opposite of a parallel circuit, where pressure is shared and flow divides.
