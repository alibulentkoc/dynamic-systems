Foundations of Dynamic Systems · Lesson 1

Dynamic Systems Everywhere

Push a heavy cart and it eases up to speed, then coasts when you stop. Step on a scale and it reads your weight almost at once. Those two everyday objects sit at opposite ends of one spectrum — and telling them apart is the whole idea behind everything in this course. A dynamic system responds over time and remembers; a static one just mirrors the input right now.

The big idea A static system's output depends only on the input at this instant. A dynamic system's output depends on the input's whole recent history — because it stores energy, and stored energy is memory. The compact summary of that stored history is the system's state. Finding and using the state is what this entire course is about.

Step 1 · Static vs. dynamic

Memory is the whole distinction

The bathroom scale is nearly static: press with a force, it shows that force; remove it, the reading is gone. It stores almost no energy, so it has almost no memory. The shopping cart is dynamic: how fast it's moving depends not on how hard you push this instant, but on how hard you've pushed over the last few seconds. That memory has a physical name — momentum — and it's stored in the cart's mass.

static → output tracks input now dynamic → output remembers history memory = stored energy state = compact summary of that history

Step 2 · A first model — the "chase"

Output always trying to catch its input

Put the lightest possible math on the idea. Imagine a system whose output y is always trying to catch up to its input u — the bigger the gap, the faster it moves:

dy/dt = (1/τ) · ( u − y )   // rate of change = gap ÷ time constant

Read it symbol by symbol: dy/dt is how fast the output moves right now; u − y is the gap between target and current value — the engine of the motion; and τ (tau), the time constant in seconds, sets the pace. Small τ → the 1/τ out front is large → the system snaps toward the target (a nimble drone). Large τ → sluggish, easing in slowly (a loaded freight elevator, or a big room warming up).

Step 3 · See it — the same push, two systems

Static jumps; dynamic chases

Below, the same input is fed to both systems: a sudden push at t = 1 s. The static system mirrors it instantly; the dynamic one chases it with time constant τ. Drag τ and watch the personality change; switch to a pulse (push then release) to see the dynamic system lag on the way down too — that lag is the memory.

input u(t) static output dynamic output (chase)

Three things to notice in the teal (dynamic) curve: it doesn't jump — it curves, and that curve is the memory; it rises fastest at the start, when the gap u − y is biggest, then eases off; and after a while it settles on the target and looks static again. Dynamics matter most during change. After a push, the dynamic output reaches about 63% of the way in exactly one time constant, and is essentially settled after about 4τ.

Try this Set τ very small (0.2 s). The teal curve nearly matches the input — the system is so fast it looks almost static. That's a deep preview: "static" is just "dynamic, but too fast to notice on your timescale." A steel beam is static to your hand but very much dynamic to a vibration sensor.

Step 4 · The same idea, across real machines

Once you look, lag-and-remember behavior is everywhere: a car body keeps bobbing after the wheel clears a bump; an elevator accelerates, cruises, decelerates; a drone climbs toward a commanded height; a room warms gradually; and a hydraulic cylinder builds speed as oil flows in — with the trapped oil acting slightly springy, storing energy. Every one takes an input, stores some energy, and releases its response over time. No storage, no dynamics.

Key takeaways

  • A static system's output depends only on the present input; a dynamic system's output depends on the recent history of the input.
  • Dynamics come from stored energy — momentum in a mass, charge in a capacitor, compressed oil in a cylinder. No storage → no memory → no dynamics.
  • The state is the compact summary of that stored history — often position and velocity. Finding and using the state is what this whole course is about.
  • The first-order "chase," dy/dt = (1/τ)(u − y), captures a huge amount of real behavior; the time constant τ sets how eager the chase is (63% in one τ, settled by ~4τ).
  • "Static" is often just "dynamic, but fast relative to how we're watching."

Quick-check quiz

1 · In one sentence, what separates a static from a dynamic system?

A static system's output depends only on the input at the present instant; a dynamic system's output depends on the recent history of the input — it has memory.

2 · Scale vs. shopping cart — which is more "dynamic," and what does it have that the other lacks?

The shopping cart. It stores energy (momentum in its mass) and therefore remembers past pushes; the scale stores almost no energy, so it reflects only the present force.

3 · What physical quantity stores the "memory" of a moving shopping cart?

Momentum, stored in the cart's mass (equivalently its velocity — part of the cart's state).

4 · Two drones respond to a climb command — one snaps up, one drifts up slowly. Which has the larger τ?

The slow drone. A larger τ makes the 1/τ factor smaller, so the system chases its target more slowly.

5 · Why is a steel beam "static to your hand but dynamic to a vibration sensor"?

Nothing about the beam changes — the timescale of observation does. Your hand acts over tenths of a second, on which the beam's vibrations are far too fast and tiny to feel, so it seems rigid. A vibration sensor watches microsecond-to-millisecond behavior, on which the beam's stored elastic energy produces real, measurable ringing.

Exercises

C1 · Rate of change right now

In dy/dt = (1/τ)(u − y), with u = 1, y = 0.4, τ = 2 s: dy/dt = ½(1 − 0.4) = ½(0.6) = 0.3 per second.

C2 · One small step forward

With Δt = 0.1 s, ynew ≈ y + Δt·(dy/dt) = 0.4 + 0.1×0.3 = 0.43. The output crept closer to the target of 1 — exactly what "chasing" looks like, one small step at a time (this is the update rule the demo integrates).

C3 · Where does it settle?

dy/dt = 0 requires u − y = 0, i.e. y = u = 1. The system stops changing only when it has fully caught up to its target — its steady state. That's why the dynamic curve flattens at the input level.

D1 · Design: one fixed τ can't satisfy both complaints

A single fixed τ forces one personality everywhere: small τ is quick but nervous; large τ is calm but slow — you can't get "quick when far, calm when close" from one constant. The fix is a scheduled/adaptive time constant: let τ depend on a measured condition — the error size (calmer when close, snappier when far), altitude/ground proximity (gentle near the ground, faster up high), or payload/mass. Any answer tying τ to a sensible measurable quantity, with the right direction of effect, is correct. You've just reasoned your way to adaptive control.
