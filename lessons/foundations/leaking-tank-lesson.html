The Leaking Tank

A tank drains at a rate proportional to its water height. We follow one path — phenomenon → intuition → equation → solution → interpretation — with the schematic and the live response sitting right beside the algebra at every step.

① The scenario ② The state ③ Build the equation ④ Why exponential ⑤ Solve & verify ⑥ Height ↔ volume ⑦ Takeaways

1 The scenario

A phenomenon you can picture before any math appears.

A water tank of cross-sectional area A=5 m² sits with a leak at the bottom. Nothing is being pumped in — the tank is only emptying. The single rule we're given is that the outflow is proportional to the water height: taller column, faster leak.

2 Choosing the state variable

The first modeling decision — and the one most people get subtly wrong.

A dynamic model needs a state: the quantity whose rate of change we'll track. Here the tempting choice is volume, but there's a better one. The leak law is written in terms of height, so height is the variable the physics already speaks. Choose h(t) and the equation writes itself; choose volume and you owe an extra conversion.

The classic trap. Writing dVdt=V0−0.2V makes two errors at once: V0 is a starting amount, not an inflow rate (a sealed leaking tank has inflow = 0), and the leak is tied to height, not volume. Initial conditions never live inside the balance — they're applied afterward.

4 Why the response is exponential

Not "because of the minus sign" — the sign only picks the direction.

The equation dhdt=−0.04h has the right-hand side proportional to the state itself. That proportionality is what creates the exponential. The general law is dydt=ky, and the sign of k alone decides the fate: grow, hold, or decay.

Full worked solution — separation of variables (and the Laplace route)

From dhdt=−kh, gather like variables and integrate:

dhh=−k dt⟶lnh=−kt+C

Exponentiate and fix the constant with h(0)=h0:

h(t)=h0e−kt

Laplace: sH−h0=−kH ⇒ H(s)=h0/(s+k), whose inverse is the same exponential. Both roads meet.

6 Height ↔ volume: the same system twice

If you insist on volume, the geometry link converts the coefficient — but not the time constant.

Using V=Ah, substituting h=V/A gives dVdt=−0.2VA=−0.04V. Same coefficient — because a linear rescale can't change how fast the system settles. Only the vertical scale differs.

Takeaways

  • State follows the physics. The leak law was written in height, so h is the state; volume forces an avoidable conversion.
  • Balance, then simplify. Accumulation = inflow − outflow. Dividing by A folds geometry into the coefficient c/A.
  • Proportionality makes the exponential; the sign chooses growth or decay. dydt=ky ⟶ y0ekt.
  • The time constant is invariant. τ = A/c = 25 s whether you track height or volume — a property of the system, not the yardstick.
Quick check 1 — reading the rate

With k = 0.04 s⁻¹, about how long until the height falls to roughly a third of its start?

One time constant, τ = 1/k = 25 s, brings h to 1/e ≈ 37% of h₀ — roughly a third. Half is reached earlier, at the half-life ln2/k ≈ 17.3 s.
Quick check 2 — spotting the error

A student writes dV/dt = V₀ − 0.2V. Name the two mistakes.

(1) V₀ is an initial condition, not an inflow — the sealed leaking tank has inflow = 0. (2) The leak is 0.2·h, not 0.2·V; in volume coordinates it becomes 0.2·V/A = 0.04·V.
Exercise — a tank with inflow

Now a pump adds a constant Qin = 0.1 m³/s while the same leak drains it. Write the ODE for h and find the steady-state height.

Balance: A·dh/dt = Qin − 0.2h ⟶ 5·dh/dt = 0.1 − 0.2h ⟶ dh/dt = 0.02 − 0.04h. Steady state sets dh/dt = 0: hss = 0.02/0.04 = 0.5 m. The response is still first-order with τ = 25 s, now settling toward 0.5 m instead of 0.
