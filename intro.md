# Foundations of Dynamic Systems

### From Real-World Physics to Laplace Transforms and State Space

Almost everything that matters in engineering *changes over time*. A car speeds up.
A drone corrects its altitude. A hydraulic cylinder extends. A washing machine drum
spins up, wobbles, and settles. The branch of engineering that studies **how things
change over time, and how we can shape that change on purpose**, is the study of
**dynamic systems** — and it is the foundation of robotics, mechatronics, fluid power,
and physical AI.

This short course is your on-ramp. It is deliberately **intuition-first**. We build a
physical picture of what is happening *before* we write a single equation, and every
equation you meet gets explained in plain English.

## Who this is for

Senior undergraduate engineering students, beginning graduate students, and practicing
engineers who have **little or no prior exposure** to control systems or Laplace
transforms. If you remember basic calculus (derivatives and integrals) and Newton's
laws, you have enough to start.

## What you'll be able to do by the end

- Explain **why** engineers use differential equations, and read one physically.
- Explain **why** the Laplace transform was invented and what it buys you.
- Look at a **transfer function** and describe the real system it represents.
- Say what **poles and zeros** mean *physically* — not just algebraically.
- Understand what a **state-space** model is and why modern engineering leans on it.
- Recognize the *same* dynamics showing up in **mechanical, electrical, and hydraulic**
  systems — the insight that makes fluid power feel natural.

## How the course is built

Eight core lessons (20–40 minutes each) plus two mini-projects, roughly **5–7 hours**
total — a weekend, or a warm-up before a heavier controls or fluid-power course.

Every lesson follows the same rhythm:

1. **A physical phenomenon** you can picture or touch.
2. **Intuition** — what's really going on.
3. **An engineering example** from a real machine.
4. **The mathematical model** — introduced only once you already understand it.
5. **Interpretation** — reading the math back into the physical world.

And every lesson ends with key takeaways, exercises with full worked solutions, a short
conceptual quiz, an **interactive coding demo** you can play with, and — where it helps —
a **household experiment** using things like rubber bands, a ruler, or your phone's
accelerometer.

A recurring thread runs through the whole course: **hydraulics**. We keep returning to
the hydraulic cylinder, fluid stiffness, pressure-as-force, and resonance, so that by
the end fluid power feels like a special case of ideas you already own.
